\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This project investigates Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) as mesh-free solvers for option-pricing partial differential equations. The starting point is the connection between the Black--Scholes equation and the heat equation: the terminal payoff of a European option is propagated backward in time by a diffusion-type PDE, producing a smooth continuation-value surface from a non-smooth payoff kink. A fully connected neural network is trained to approximate this pricing function while satisfying the governing PDE, terminal payoff condition, and boundary conditions through automatic differentiation.

The framework is first tested on the Black--Scholes equation, where a closed-form solution provides an analytical benchmark. The final Black--Scholes PINN achieves a pointwise price of $6.927$ at $S=K=100$ and $\tau=0.5$, compared with the analytical value $6.889$, corresponding to an absolute error of $0.0384$ and a relative error of approximately $0.56\%$. The model also produces meaningful Greeks and a small local PDE residual of $2.75\times 10^{-3}$.

The method is then extended to the Heston stochastic-volatility model, where the price depends on asset price, variance, and time to maturity. This introduces an additional state variable and a mixed derivative term in the PDE. The final Heston PINN, evaluated on the slice $v=v_0=0.04$, predicts a price of $6.807$ against a semi-analytical Heston benchmark of $6.837$, giving an absolute error of $0.0302$ and a relative error of approximately $0.44\%$. Although the price fit remains strong, the larger PDE residual and more volatile loss history show that the Heston PDE is substantially harder to satisfy globally.

Finally, the Heston PINN is adapted to an inverse calibration problem in which the parameters $\kappa$, $\theta$, $\xi$, and $\rho$ are treated as trainable variables and inferred from noisy synthetic option prices. The inverse PINN fits the observed prices very accurately, achieving mean absolute error $0.0286$, median relative error approximately $0.02\%$, and $R^2 \approx 1.000$. However, the recovered parameter vector is not structurally correct: in particular, the correlation parameter converges to a large positive value despite the true value being negative. The results therefore show that PINNs are promising differentiable pricing engines, but that inverse Heston calibration remains ill-conditioned and requires stronger identification constraints, richer data, and more careful regularization.
\end{abstract}
