\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The Black--Scholes PDE underpins trillions of dollars of derivatives but rests on a constant-volatility assumption that markets violate every minute through implied-volatility smiles and skews. Stochastic-volatility extensions such as the Heston model close this gap in theory, yet they drive classical grid-based solvers into a curse of dimensionality and turn calibration into a notoriously ill-conditioned inverse problem. In this project we use Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) to embed the option-pricing PDE directly into the training loss, so the learned price surface is constrained to satisfy the underlying financial model rather than merely interpolating data. We build the framework in three stages: a constant-volatility Black--Scholes benchmark with a closed-form reference, the three-dimensional Heston PDE benchmarked against a Fourier-quadrature reference, and an inverse Heston problem in which the structural parameters $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)$ are made trainable and recovered from $200$ noisy synthetic prices.

On Black--Scholes our PINN reproduces the analytical call price at the at-the-money point to within $0.56\%$ ($V_{\mathrm{PINN}}=6.927$ vs.\ $V_{\mathrm{BS}}=6.889$, $12$\,min on a single GPU). On Heston the same framework prices the full surface to within $0.44\%$ of the Fourier benchmark ($6.807$ vs.\ $6.837$, $25$\,min). The inverse case is more revealing: our calibrated network fits the observed prices essentially perfectly ($R^{2}\approx 0.999999$, median relative pricing error $\approx 0.02\%$), yet the recovered correlation has the wrong sign ($\hat{\rho}=+0.994$ against true $\rho=-0.7$). A near-perfect price fit therefore does not imply structural-parameter recovery: $\rho$ enters the Heston PDE only through the mixed term and is identifiable only from the implied-volatility skew that sparse prices cannot carry. PINNs emerge as strong differentiable forward pricers across both models, but the inverse problem still demands economically motivated regularisation, denser strike-maturity grids, and implied-volatility loss formulations.
\end{abstract}
