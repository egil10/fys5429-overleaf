\begin{abstract}
\noindent
\textbf{Topic.} Option pricing reduces, after a standard change of variables, to a heat equation; the same parabolic structure governs the higher-dimensional Heston stochastic-volatility model. Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) embed such equations directly into the training objective of a neural network, providing a mesh-free, differentiable surrogate that yields prices and Greeks simultaneously through automatic differentiation.
\textbf{Challenge.} Classical grid-based pricers scale poorly to multi-factor models, and inverse calibration of structural parameters from observed prices is an ill-conditioned nonlinear problem. It is not clear how well a single PINN architecture can handle the forward and inverse versions of both Black--Scholes and Heston under realistic compute budgets.
\textbf{Method.} A fully connected network with parameters $\boldsymbol{w}$ is trained on Google Colab H100/A100 GPUs to minimise a composite loss combining a PDE residual, a terminal payoff condition, and asset-price boundary conditions; derivatives are obtained by reverse-mode AD. For the inverse problem, the structural Heston parameters $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)$ are made trainable and reparameterised through softplus / $\tanh$ bijections, then optimised jointly with $\boldsymbol{w}$ by a two-stage Adam + L-BFGS schedule with a Feller penalty.
\textbf{Results.} On the closed-form Black--Scholes call, the PINN attains $V_{\mathrm{PINN}}=6.927$ versus $V_{\mathrm{BS}}=6.889$ at $(S,\tau)=(100,0.5)$ -- an absolute error of $0.0384$ ($0.56\%$ relative) with a local PDE residual of $2.75\times 10^{-3}$ ($50{,}000$ epochs, 12\,min 8\,s). On the three-dimensional Heston PDE, the PINN reaches $6.807$ against the semi-analytical Fourier-quadrature benchmark $6.837$, an absolute error of $0.0302$ ($0.44\%$ relative; 24\,min 40\,s). The inverse Heston PINN fits $200$ noisy synthetic prices with mean absolute error $0.0286$, median relative error $\approx 0.02\%$, and $R^2 \approx 0.999999$, but recovers $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)=(1.59,0.0266,0.104,0.994)$ against the true generating values $(2,0.04,0.3,-0.7)$ -- most strikingly, the correlation has the \emph{wrong sign}.
\textbf{Implications.} The price--parameter dissociation reflects the fact that $\rho$ enters the Heston PDE only through the mixed-derivative term and is therefore identifiable only through skew information that $200$ sparsely placed prices cannot carry: PINNs are already strong differentiable forward pricing engines, but inverse calibration is genuinely ill-conditioned at this data density and will require denser strike--maturity grids, economically motivated regularisation of $(\xi,\rho)$, and loss functions formulated in implied-volatility rather than price space.
\end{abstract}
