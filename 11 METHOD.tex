\section{Methods}\label{section:methods}

This section develops the mathematical models, the neural-network function class, the automatic-differentiation pipeline, and the collocation, training, and calibration strategies used in the project. The objective is to approximate the option-price surfaces $V(S,\tau)$ and $V(S,v,\tau)$ by training Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) \parencite{raissi2019physics,karniadakis2021physics,cuomo2022scientific} that simultaneously satisfy the governing pricing PDEs and respect the terminal payoff and asset-price boundary conditions. The Black--Scholes model \parencite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational} is used first as a benchmark with a closed-form solution, before the same framework is extended to the Heston stochastic-volatility model \parencite{heston1993closed} and to an inverse calibration in which Heston parameters are recovered jointly with the network weights from noisy synthetic data.

\subsection{Notation}\label{subsec:notation}

Because the project combines two financial models with a neural network, several Greek letters carry context-dependent meanings; \cref{tab:notation} fixes the conventions used throughout. The neural-network weights and biases are collected into a single parameter vector $\boldsymbol{w}$, so $\theta$ is reserved for the Heston long-run variance, and the Black--Scholes rate sensitivity is written $\rho_r$ to distinguish it from the Heston correlation $\rho$. Loss components carry subscripts identifying their role (PDE residual, initial condition, boundary condition, data, Feller penalty) and are weighted by non-negative coefficients $\lambda_{\bullet}$. The PDE residual operator acting on the network output is denoted $\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}$.

\subsection{Pricing PDEs and closed-form benchmarks}\label{subsec:pdes}

Under the standard risk-neutral assumptions of \textcite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational}, an asset following geometric Brownian motion $dS_t=rS_t\,dt+\sigma S_t\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$ yields, by It\^o's lemma \parencite{itoh1944stochastic} and a $\Delta$-hedging argument, the Black--Scholes PDE
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bs_pde}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}+\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}+rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}-rV=0,
\end{equation}
on $(S,t)\in\mathbb{R}_+\times[0,T)$, with European call terminal data $V(S,T)=\max(S-K,0)$. The same equation arises probabilistically through the Feynman--Kac representation $V(S,t)=e^{-r(T-t)}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\Psi(S_T)\mid S_t=S]$ \parencite{shreve2004stochastic}, which connects the deterministic PDE to its Monte Carlo dual \parencite{glasserman2003monte} and to deep-BSDE solvers \parencite{han2018solving,e2017deep,beck2020deepbsde}. After the change of variables $x=\ln(S/K)$, $\tau=T-t$ and a rescaling of $V$, equation \eqref{eq:bs_pde} reduces to the canonical heat equation \eqref{eq:heat-equation} \parencite{wilmott1995mathematics,capinski2012blackscholes}, so option pricing is literally backward diffusion of the kinked payoff into a smooth continuation-value surface. The PINN does not exploit this reduction explicitly, but it explains why $V$ is $C^{\infty}$ on $\tau>0$ and motivates smooth, twice-differentiable activations.

For PINN training the problem is recast on $(S,\tau)$ with $\tau=T-t$, giving the initial condition $V(S,0)=\max(S-K,0)$ and boundary conditions $V(0,\tau)=0$ and $V(S_{\max},\tau)\approx S_{\max}-Ke^{-r\tau}$ on the truncated domain $S\in[0,S_{\max}]$ with $S_{\max}=300$ and $\tau\in[0,T_{\max}]$ with $T_{\max}=1$. The closed-form European call $C=SN(d_1)-Ke^{-r\tau}N(d_2)$ with $d_1=[\ln(S/K)+(r+\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\tau]/(\sigma\sqrt{\tau})$, $d_2=d_1-\sigma\sqrt{\tau}$ \parencite{black1973pricing} serves as exact ground truth.

The Heston model \parencite{heston1993closed} replaces constant volatility with a CIR variance process \parencite{cox1985cir}, $dS_t=rS_t\,dt+\sqrt{v_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^S$ and $dv_t=\kappa(\theta-v_t)\,dt+\xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^v$ with $dW_t^S\,dW_t^v=\rho\,dt$. The Feller condition $2\kappa\theta>\xi^2$ \parencite{feller1951two} prevents $v$ from hitting zero. Repeating the $\Delta$-hedging argument with two noise sources yields the three-dimensional pricing PDE \parencite{ekstrom2010bs,rouah2013heston}
\begin{align}\label{eq:heston_pde}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
    &+\tfrac{1}{2}vS^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}
    +\rho\xi vS\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S\partial v}
    +\tfrac{1}{2}\xi^2 v\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial v^2} \notag\\
    &+rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}+\kappa(\theta-v)\frac{\partial V}{\partial v}-rV=0,
\end{align}
whose structural novelty is the mixed-derivative term $\rho\xi vS\,V_{Sv}$ encoding the leverage-skew correlation \parencite{gatheral2018volatility}. This term is precisely the one that makes the inverse problem in \cref{subsec:inverse} ill-conditioned. The Heston PDE admits no elementary closed form, but \textcite{heston1993closed} derived the semi-analytical Fourier representation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heston_cf_price}
    C(S_0,v_0,\tau)=S_0 P_1-Ke^{-r\tau}P_2,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heston_pj}
    P_j=\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty}\mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-i\phi\ln K}f_j(\phi;S_0,v_0,\tau)}{i\phi}\right]d\phi,
\end{equation}
with $f_j$ the Heston characteristic function. In this work \eqref{eq:heston_pj} is evaluated by adaptive Gauss--Kronrod quadrature on $\phi\in[10^{-4},100]$ via \texttt{scipy.integrate.quad}; this is the canonical reference underlying the COS \parencite{fang2008cos} and FFT \parencite{carr1999fft,albrecher2007heston} alternatives. \Cref{fig:heston_process_panel} in the appendix shows the simulated GBM, Heston asset, and CIR variance paths used to sanity-check the collocation domain.

\subsection{PINN formulation}\label{subsec:pinn}

The unknown pricing function is represented by a fully connected feed-forward network in PyTorch \parencite{paszke2019pytorch}. With $L$ hidden layers the network is the composition
\begin{align}\label{eq:network}
    \mathbf{z}^{(0)} &= \widetilde{\mathbf{x}},\notag\\
    \mathbf{z}^{(\ell)} &= \phi\!\big(W^{(\ell)}\mathbf{z}^{(\ell-1)}+\mathbf{b}^{(\ell)}\big),\quad \ell=1,\ldots,L,\notag\\
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}) &= W^{(L+1)}\mathbf{z}^{(L)}+\mathbf{b}^{(L+1)},
\end{align}
where $\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}=(S/S_{\max},v/v_{\max},\tau/T_{\max})$ normalises every input to $\mathcal{O}(1)$ for stable conditioning \parencite{glorot2010xavier,goodfellow2016deep}. The activation $\phi$ is $\tanh$ for the forward problems and the sinusoidal SIREN activation of \textcite{sitzmann2020siren} for the inverse problem; both are $C^{\infty}$, which is essential because the residual involves $V_S$, $V_\tau$, $V_{SS}$, and for Heston also $V_{Sv}$ and $V_{vv}$. \texttt{softplus} was tested in the architecture sweep but consistently lost to $\tanh$ (\cref{section:result}); ReLU is unsuitable because its second derivative is zero or undefined almost everywhere \parencite{cuomo2022scientific}. Weights are initialised by Xavier-normal \parencite{glorot2010xavier} for $\tanh$ and by the SIREN scheme \parencite{sitzmann2020siren} for sinusoidal layers; biases are zero.

\paragraph{Theoretical footing.} Universal approximation \parencite{cybenko1989approximation,hornik1989multilayer,pinkus1999approximation} guarantees that a sufficiently wide network can represent the true solution on a compact set. The PINN requirement is stronger than pointwise approximation, however, because the residual involves first- and second-order partial derivatives, which is the second reason smooth activations are essential. Convergence and generalisation analyses tailored to PINNs on parabolic problems \parencite{shin2020convergence,mishra2022estimates} bound the squared $L^2$ error of the trained network as $\mathcal{O}(N_f^{-1/2})$ plus an irreducible optimisation gap. The Monte Carlo interpretation of $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}$ as the empirical estimator of the population residual $\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mu}[|\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x})|^2]$ under the collocation distribution $\mu$ \parencite{raissi2019physics,sirignano2018dgm} links the PINN error to the variance of an underlying Monte Carlo estimator and provides one of the few rigorous generalisation bounds available for PINNs. For the forward Heston problem this places the experiment at $N_f=10^4$ in a regime where the statistical part of the error is small relative to the gradient-pathology optimisation gap of \textcite{wang2021understanding,wang2022expert,krishnapriyan2021characterizing}.

A PINN trains $\boldsymbol{w}$ to satisfy a composite loss \parencite{raissi2019physics,raissi2017physics1}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pinn_loss}
    \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{w})=\lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}+\lambda_{\mathrm{ic}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}+\lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}},
\end{equation}
with
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}} &= \frac{1}{N_f}\sum_{i=1}^{N_f}\big|\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}_i)\big|^2,\\
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}  &= \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{ic}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{ic}}}\big|V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S_i,0)-\max(S_i-K,0)\big|^2,\\
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}  &= \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{bc}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{bc}}}\big|V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{bc}}_i)-g(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{bc}}_i)\big|^2,
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ is obtained by substituting $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ and its reverse-mode AD derivatives \parencite{baydin2018automatic} into \eqref{eq:bs_pde} or \eqref{eq:heston_pde}, and $g$ encodes the asset-price boundaries. Listing~\ref{lst:residual} shows how the Heston residual is assembled with six \texttt{torch.autograd.grad} calls; \texttt{create\_graph=True} is essential so that the residual itself remains differentiable when backpropagating $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}$ into $\boldsymbol{w}$ and, in the inverse problem, into the latent Heston parameters. The mixed second derivative $V_{Sv}$ is obtained as the variance-direction gradient of $V_S$, the only graph-safe order under reverse-mode AD when $S$ and $v$ enter on different branches.

\begin{lstlisting}[
style=pythonappendix,
caption={Heston PDE residual assembled by reverse-mode AD.},
label={lst:residual}
]
V = model(S, v, tau)
g  = torch.ones_like(V)
V_S   = grad(V, S,   grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_v   = grad(V, v,   grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_tau = grad(V, tau, grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_SS  = grad(V_S, S, grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_vv  = grad(V_v, v, grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_Sv  = grad(V_S, v, grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
R = V_tau - (0.5*v*S**2*V_SS
             + rho*xi*v*S*V_Sv
             + 0.5*xi**2*v*V_vv
             + r*S*V_S
             + kappa*(theta - v)*V_v
             - r*V)
loss_pde = (R**2).mean()
\end{lstlisting}

The relative gradient magnitudes of the three loss components are typically very imbalanced \parencite{wang2021understanding,wang2022expert,krishnapriyan2021characterizing}: at initialisation the unfitted payoff produces $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}\sim 10^{3}$, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}\sim 10^{4}$ (Heston only), while the residual evaluated on the near-zero output network is $\sim 10^{-2}$, so the gradient of $\mathcal{L}$ is dominated by the boundary and initial terms before the PDE constraint can act. This is the dominant source of PINN training instability and the reason the weights $\lambda_{\bullet}$ are tuned by grid search (\cref{section:result}). PINNs sit within a broader family of deep-learning PDE solvers including the Deep Galerkin Method \parencite{sirignano2018dgm}, deep-BSDE schemes \parencite{han2018solving,e2017deep,beck2020deepbsde}, Kolmogorov-PDE methods \parencite{beck2021deep}, and emerging basis architectures such as Kolmogorov--Arnold networks \parencite{liu2024kan}.

\subsection{Inverse calibration}\label{subsec:inverse}

In the inverse problem the four Heston parameters $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)$ are promoted to trainable variables and recovered jointly with $\boldsymbol{w}$ from $N_m=200$ noisy synthetic prices, with the same physics losses retained. The augmented loss is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse_loss}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{inv}}=\lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}+\lambda_{\mathrm{ic}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}+\lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}+\lambda_{\mathrm{data}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}+\lambda_{\mathrm{Feller}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Feller}},
\end{equation}
with $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}=N_m^{-1}\sum_i|V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S_i,v_i,\tau_i)-V_i^{\mathrm{market}}|^2$ and the soft Feller penalty
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feller_loss}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Feller}}=\big[\mathrm{ReLU}(\xi^2-2\kappa\theta)\big]^2,
\end{equation}
which vanishes whenever $2\kappa\theta\ge\xi^2$ \parencite{feller1951two,cox1985cir}. The synthetic observations are produced from true parameters $(\kappa^\star,\theta^\star,\xi^\star,\rho^\star)=(2.0,0.04,0.3,-0.7)$ via \eqref{eq:heston_cf_price} with additive Gaussian noise of standard deviation $0.5$.

Adam updates the trainable parameters without constraint, which would routinely push the Heston parameters into nonphysical regions ($\kappa\le0$ or $|\rho|\ge 1$). To prevent this, the four parameters are stored as \emph{unconstrained} latent variables $\tilde{\bullet}\in\mathbb{R}$ and exposed through smooth bijective transforms
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reparam}
    \kappa=\mathrm{softplus}(\tilde\kappa),\quad \theta=\mathrm{softplus}(\tilde\theta),\quad \xi=\mathrm{softplus}(\tilde\xi),\quad \rho=\tanh(\tilde\rho),
\end{equation}
so that the PDE residual, Feller penalty, and data loss are always evaluated at physically admissible values. Gradients flow through the transforms by chain rule. This single design choice keeps the Feller penalty at machine zero throughout training and is implemented in Listing~\ref{lst:reparam}.

\begin{lstlisting}[
style=pythonappendix,
caption={Constrained Heston parameters as differentiable properties (\texttt{invpinn.py}).},
label={lst:reparam}
]
class INVPINN(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, ..., kappa_init=1., theta_init=.1,
                 xi_init=.5, rho_init=0.):
        super().__init__()
        self._raw_kappa = nn.Parameter(_inv_softplus(
            torch.tensor([kappa_init])))
        self._raw_theta = nn.Parameter(_inv_softplus(
            torch.tensor([theta_init])))
        self._raw_xi    = nn.Parameter(_inv_softplus(
            torch.tensor([xi_init])))
        self._raw_rho   = nn.Parameter(_inv_tanh(
            torch.tensor([max(-.999, min(.999, rho_init))])))
    @property
    def kappa(self): return F.softplus(self._raw_kappa)
    @property
    def theta(self): return F.softplus(self._raw_theta)
    @property
    def xi(self):    return F.softplus(self._raw_xi)
    @property
    def rho(self):   return torch.tanh(self._raw_rho)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Training}\label{subsec:training}

All optimisation is reverse-mode AD on the PyTorch graph \parencite{baydin2018automatic,paszke2019pytorch}; no finite-difference stencil is constructed at any stage. Adam \parencite{kingma2015adam} with cosine-annealed learning rate \parencite{loshchilov2017sgdr}, $\eta_n=\eta_{\min}+\tfrac{1}{2}(\eta_0-\eta_{\min})(1+\cos(n\pi/E))$ and $\eta_{\min}=10^{-6}$, drives Phase~1 training. The global gradient norm is clipped at $1$ \parencite{pascanu2013difficulty} for stability under the imbalanced PINN landscape. For the inverse problem, an L-BFGS quasi-Newton refinement \parencite{liu1989limited} follows the Adam phase with a strong-Wolfe line search (history size $50$, tolerance $10^{-5}$), and clipping is disabled because it interferes with the curvature estimates. \Cref{alg:pinn} summarises the unified procedure; \cref{tab:nn_architectures} records the final configurations from the hyperparameter sweeps of \cref{section:result}.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp!]
\caption{PINN training: forward and (optionally) inverse}\label{alg:pinn}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require collocation $\mathcal{D}_f,\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{ic}},\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{bc}}$; if inverse, $\mathcal{D}_m$; weights $\lambda_\bullet$; Adam epochs $E_1$, LBFGS epochs $E_2$; LRs $\eta_0,\eta_{\mathrm{H}}$
\State Initialise $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ with activation-matched scheme; if inverse, latent $\tilde\kappa,\tilde\theta,\tilde\xi,\tilde\rho$ from guesses via \eqref{eq:reparam}
\For{$n=1,\ldots,E_1$ (Adam)}
    \State Forward pass: $V\leftarrow V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}_f)$; AD for first/second derivatives ($V_{Sv}$ too for Heston)
    \State $\mathcal{R}\leftarrow$ strong-form residual of \eqref{eq:bs_pde}/\eqref{eq:heston_pde}; assemble $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde,ic,bc}}$, plus $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}},\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Feller}}$ if inverse
    \State Backpropagate; clip $\|g\|_2\le 1$; Adam step with $\eta_n$
\EndFor
\For{$n=1,\ldots,E_2$ (LBFGS, inverse only)}
    \State closure: zero grads, recompute $\mathcal{L}$, backprop \emph{without} clipping; strong-Wolfe step
\EndFor
\State \Return $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ (and calibrated $\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho$ if inverse)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Collocation, testing, and Greeks}\label{subsec:collocation}

Collocation points are drawn from a mixture distribution combining a uniform background with concentrations near the strike, near expiry, and (for Heston) near the initial variance level, since the kinked payoff $\max(S-K,0)$ propagates the strongest curvature backward from $\tau=0$ \parencite{lu2021deepxde,wang2022expert}. \Cref{tab:collocation} summarises the three datasets used in this project. For the forward Heston network the boundary loss imposes both $V(0,v,\tau)=0$ and the upper Dirichlet $V(S_{\max},v,\tau)\approx S_{\max}-Ke^{-r\tau}$; for the inverse network only the lower boundary $S=0$ is enforced, the upper-$S$ and variance edges being absorbed by the price-data signal.

Each trained PINN is validated against four exit criteria. \emph{(i) Closed-form Black--Scholes benchmark.} With $K=100,r=0.05,\sigma=0.2$, the analytical call $C=SN(d_1)-Ke^{-r\tau}N(d_2)$ with $d_1=[\ln(S/K)+(r+\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\tau]/(\sigma\sqrt{\tau})$, $d_2=d_1-\sigma\sqrt{\tau}$ \parencite{black1973pricing} gives $C=6.888729$ at the at-the-money point $(S,\tau)=(100,0.5)$ and is used pointwise in the absolute-error figures. \emph{(ii) Semi-analytical Heston benchmark.} Equations \eqref{eq:heston_cf_price}--\eqref{eq:heston_pj} are evaluated by adaptive Gauss--Kronrod quadrature on the truncated frequency interval $[10^{-4},100]$, with the integrand expressed in non-discounted characteristic form and an early-payoff branch for $\tau\le 10^{-4}$ to avoid the $1/(i\phi)$ singularity. Faster alternatives (the FFT method of \textcite{carr1999fft}, the COS expansion of \textcite{fang2008cos}, and the ``little Heston trap'' regularisation of \textcite{albrecher2007heston}) agree to within $10^{-6}$ but were not needed at the grid sizes used here. \emph{(iii) Put--call parity.} The trained call surface combined with $C-P=S-Ke^{-r\tau}$ implies a put surface; deviations from the analytical put price remain below $10^{-2}$ at the at-the-money point. \emph{(iv) AD versus finite differences.} Reverse-mode AD derivatives at the evaluation point agree with $h=10^{-3}$ central differences for $V_S, V_{SS}, V_{Sv}$ to four significant figures, ruling out a wrong-residual bug. An independent $10^5$-path Monte Carlo cross-check \parencite{glasserman2003monte} using the log-Euler GBM scheme and the reflection-Euler Heston scheme of \textcite{andersen2008simple,lord2010comparison} reproduced both references to within statistical noise; these are not reported separately because they reproduce the benchmarks to within Monte Carlo error, but they protect the evaluation against systematic bugs in either reference.

Greeks are obtained directly by differentiating the trained surrogate, with no bump-and-revalue. \Cref{tab:greeks_expressions} lists every sensitivity computed in this project. For the Black--Scholes PINN, only $(S,\tau)$-derivatives are accessible by AD: Delta, Theta, Gamma, Charm, Speed, and Color. Volatility/rate Greeks (Vega, Vomma, Vanna, $\rho_r$, $\Phi$) require a \emph{parametric} PINN with $\sigma,r,q$ as additional inputs and are reported as N/A from the present non-parametric network \parencite{lewis2025pinnsoptions}. For the Heston PINN $v$ is already an input, so the variance sensitivities Vega$_v$, Volga$_v$, Vanna$_v$, Zomma$_v$ are likewise available by AD; these should be read as variance-state sensitivities rather than direct Black--Scholes volatility sensitivities.

\subsection{Computational environment}\label{subsec:compute}

All production training was executed on Google Colab GPU runtimes \parencite{google_colab_faq}: NVIDIA H100 \parencite{nvidia_h100,choquette2023nvidia} for the Black--Scholes and inverse experiments, NVIDIA A100 \parencite{nvidia_a100} for the Heston run. Each Heston training step evaluates the network and the residual of \eqref{eq:heston_pde} at $10{,}000$ interior collocation points using one forward and six backward AD passes, giving $\mathcal{O}(7\times 10^4)$ dense-network evaluations per epoch and $\mathcal{O}(3.5\times 10^{9})$ over the full $50{,}000$-epoch run, a CPU job of several days that maps onto the Tensor Cores of \parencite{markidis2018tensor,nvidia_h100} in minutes. \Cref{tab:runtime_summary} reports wall-clock times. All runs fix Python, NumPy, and PyTorch seeds to $42$; the trained checkpoints, the loss histories, and the collocation parquet files are stored in the companion repositories \parencite{furnes2026fys5429code,furnes2026fys5429overleaf}.
