\section{Methods}\label{section:methods}

One importance of the heat equation in option pricing arises from the fact that the Black-Scholes PDE can be transformed into a diffusion equation of this form through a suitable change of variables. 

Under the standard geometric Brownian motion model for an asset price $S_t$,
\begin{equation}
    dS_t = \mu S_tdt + \sigma S_t dW_t 
\end{equation}
the value $V(S,t)$ of a European option satisfies the Black-Scholes PDE
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}-rV=0
\end{equation}

By introducing the log-price transformation
\begin{equation}
    x=\ln\left(\frac{S}{K}\right),\quad\tau=T-t
\end{equation}
together with an exponential rescaling of the dependent variable, the Black-Scholes equation can be reduced to the standard diffusion equation
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau}=\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}
\end{equation}

This connection provides both analytical insight and a natural bridge to modern numerical approaches.

\subsection{The Black-Scholes model}

The Black-Scholes model \parencite{black1973pricing} prices a European call or put option analytically under the assumption of constant volatility. Given the log-moneyness variables $d_1$ and $d_2$.
\begin{equation}
    d_1=\frac{\ln(S/K)+(r+\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}},\quad d_2=d_1-\sigma\sqrt{T}
\end{equation}
the call and put prices are 
\begin{align}
    C&=SN(d_1)-Ke^{-rT}N(d_2)\\
    P&= Ke^{-rT}N(-d_2)-SN(-d_1)
\end{align}
where $S$ is the current asset price, $K$ the strike, $r$ the risk-free rate, $\sigma$ the volatility, $T$ the time to maturity, and $N(\cdot)$ the standard normal CDF.

\subsection{The Heston model}

While the Black-Scholes model assumes constant volatility, empirical financial markets exhibit stochastic volatility dynamics. A widely used extension is the Heston stochastic volatility model, introduced by \textcite{heston1993closed}, in which both the asset price and its variance evolve as stochastic processes:
\begin{align}
    dS_t&=rS_tdt+\sqrt{v_t}S_tdW_t^S \\
    d_vt&=\kappa(\theta-v_t)dt+\xi\sqrt{v_t}dW_t^v
\end{align}
with correlation
\begin{equation}
    dW_t^sdW_t^v=\rho dt
\end{equation}

In this setting, the option price becomes a function $V(S,v,t)$ and satisfies the two-dimensional Heston PDE
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
&+ \frac{1}{2} v S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}
+ \rho \xi v S \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S \partial v}
+ \frac{1}{2} \xi^2 v \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial v^2} \notag \\
&+ rS \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
+ \kappa(\theta - v)\frac{\partial V}{\partial v}
- rV = 0.
\end{align}

Compared to the Black-Scholes equation, the Heston PDE introduces an additional spatial dimension and a mixed derivative term, making classical numerical solution methods more computationally demanding. This increased complexity makes the model particularly well-suited for physics-informed machine learning approaches.
