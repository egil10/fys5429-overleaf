\section{Methods}\label{section:methods}

This section describes the mathematical models, the neural-network function class, the automatic-differentiation procedure, and the collocation, training, and calibration strategies used in the project. The overarching goal is to approximate option-price surfaces $V(S,\tau)$ and $V(S,v,\tau)$ by training Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) \parencite{raissi2019physics,karniadakis2021physics,cuomo2022scientific} that simultaneously satisfy the governing pricing PDEs and respect terminal payoff and asset-price boundary conditions. The Black--Scholes model \parencite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational} is used first as a controlled benchmark with a closed-form solution, before extending the same framework to the higher-dimensional Heston stochastic-volatility model \parencite{heston1993closed} and finally to an inverse calibration problem in which selected Heston parameters are recovered jointly with the network weights from noisy synthetic data.

\subsection{Notation and conventions}\label{subsec:notation}

Since the project moves between two financial models and a neural-network parameterization, several Greek letters carry context-dependent meanings. To avoid ambiguity, the following conventions are used throughout. The neural-network weights and biases are collected into a single parameter vector $\boldsymbol{w}$, written in boldface, so that the symbol $\theta$ is reserved exclusively for the Heston long-run variance. Likewise, the Black--Scholes rate sensitivity (Rho) is denoted $\rho_r$ to distinguish it from the Heston Brownian correlation $\rho$. Loss-function components carry subscripts identifying their role (PDE residual, initial condition, boundary condition, market data, Feller penalty), and each is associated with a non-negative weight $\lambda_{\bullet}$. Collocation-point counts use the symbol $N$ with a subscript indicating the corresponding loss term. The PDE residual operator acting on the network output is denoted $\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}$. These conventions are summarized in \cref{tab:notation}.

\subsection{From the heat equation to option-pricing PDEs}\label{subsec:derivation}

The connection between option pricing and the physics of diffusion is not metaphorical but exact: under the standard frictionless-market assumptions of \textcite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational}, the European option value satisfies a parabolic PDE that reduces to the heat equation \eqref{eq:heat-equation} after a textbook change of variables \parencite{wilmott1995mathematics,capinski2012blackscholes,hull2018options}. We sketch the chain of reductions here because each step has a direct counterpart in the PINN formulation that follows: the stochastic differential equation determines the collocation domain, It\^o's lemma determines the PDE residual operator, and the change of variables explains the conditioning of the network's input normalization.

\paragraph{Step 1: GBM dynamics and It\^o's lemma.} Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the asset price follows the geometric Brownian motion (GBM) of \textcite{black1973pricing},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gbm}
    dS_t = r S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\end{equation}
with constant drift $r$ and volatility $\sigma$, $W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$ a standard $\mathbb{Q}$-Brownian motion, and the no-arbitrage requirement that any tradable asset's discounted price be a $\mathbb{Q}$-martingale \parencite{shreve2004stochastic,karatzas1991brownian,oksendal2003sde}. For any sufficiently smooth $V(S,t)\in C^{2,1}$, It\^o's lemma \parencite{itoh1944stochastic} gives
\begin{align}\label{eq:ito}
    dV
    &= \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}\right)dt \notag\\
    &\quad + \sigma S\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\end{align}
which already exposes the second-order spatial operator that the PINN will later be asked to reproduce by automatic differentiation.

\paragraph{Step 2: Delta-hedged portfolio and the Black--Scholes PDE.} Consider the hedged portfolio $\Pi_t = V_t - \Delta_t S_t$ with $\Delta_t = \partial V/\partial S$. Substituting \eqref{eq:gbm} and \eqref{eq:ito} into $d\Pi_t = dV_t - \Delta_t\,dS_t$ eliminates the $dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$ term and leaves a locally riskless process; equating its drift to the riskless return $r\Pi_t\,dt$ produces the Black--Scholes PDE \parencite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational,wilmott1995mathematics,hull2018options}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bs_pde}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
    + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}
    + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
    - rV = 0,
\end{equation}
on $(S,t)\in\mathbb{R}_+\times[0,T)$. The same equation arises probabilistically from the Feynman--Kac formula \parencite{feynman1948space,kac1949distributions,shreve2004stochastic}, which represents the solution as the discounted risk-neutral expectation $V(S,t)=e^{-r(T-t)}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\,\Psi(S_T)\mid S_t=S\,]$ of the payoff $\Psi$ -- a dual viewpoint that connects \eqref{eq:bs_pde} directly to Monte Carlo pricers \parencite{glasserman2003monte} and, more abstractly, to the deep-BSDE solvers of \textcite{han2018solving,e2017deep}.

\paragraph{Step 3: Reduction to the heat equation.} Introduce log-moneyness $x=\ln(S/K)$, time-to-maturity $\tau=T-t$, and the rescaled price $u(x,\tau)=e^{\alpha x+\beta\tau}V(S,t)$. Choosing $\alpha=-\tfrac{1}{2}+r/\sigma^2$ and $\beta=-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\alpha^2-r$ cancels the convection $rS\partial_S V$, the reaction $-rV$, and the drift induced by It\^o's correction, leaving the canonical heat equation \parencite{wilmott1995mathematics,capinski2012blackscholes}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bs_heat}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau} = \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}.
\end{equation}
This is the precise sense in which option pricing is diffusion: the kinked payoff $\Psi(x)=\max(Ke^{x}-K,0)$ at $\tau=0$ propagates backwards under \eqref{eq:bs_heat} into a $C^{\infty}$ continuation-value surface for $\tau>0$. The PINN does not exploit this reduction \emph{explicitly} -- it learns $V(S,\tau)$ in the original coordinates -- but the reduction explains why the surface is smooth and why a network with smooth activations is a natural ansatz.

\paragraph{Step 4: From Black--Scholes to Heston.} The constant-volatility assumption is restrictive \parencite{gatheral2018volatility,fouque2011multiscale}, and the natural extension introduces a second SDE for the variance. The Heston model \parencite{heston1993closed} replaces \eqref{eq:gbm} by the correlated Cox--Ingersoll--Ross system \parencite{cox1985cir}
\begin{align}\label{eq:heston_sde}
    dS_t &= rS_t\,dt + \sqrt{v_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^{S},\\
    dv_t &= \kappa(\theta-v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^{v},
\end{align}
with $dW_t^{S}\,dW_t^{v}=\rho\,dt$. Repeating the It\^o--hedging argument with two sources of randomness (or, equivalently, taking the discounted expectation under the variance-augmented martingale measure) yields the two-dimensional Heston PDE \eqref{eq:heston_pde} introduced below \parencite{heston1993closed,ekstrom2010bs,rouah2013heston}. The structural novelty relative to \eqref{eq:bs_pde} is the mixed derivative $\rho\xi vS\,\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial v$, which encodes the leverage correlation responsible for the implied-volatility skew \parencite{gatheral2018volatility} and which is precisely the term that the inverse PINN of Section~\ref{subsec:inverse} will struggle to identify.

\paragraph{Why this matters for the PINN.} Each step above maps onto a concrete design choice in the network. The SDE \eqref{eq:gbm} (or \eqref{eq:heston_sde}) sets the collocation domain $\mathbb{R}_+\times\mathbb{R}_+\times[0,T]$; It\^o's lemma \eqref{eq:ito} sets the differential operator whose residual is minimized; the reduction \eqref{eq:bs_heat} guarantees that the target $V$ is $C^{\infty}$ on $\tau>0$ and motivates smooth, twice-differentiable activations such as $\tanh$, GELU, and SIREN \parencite{sitzmann2020siren,cuomo2022scientific,karniadakis2021physics}; and the Feynman--Kac viewpoint links the PINN error to the variance of an underlying Monte Carlo estimator, providing one of the few rigorous generalization bounds available for PINNs \parencite{mishra2022estimates,shin2020convergence}.

\subsection{The Black--Scholes model}

The Black--Scholes model \parencite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational} provides a closed-form benchmark for European option prices under the assumptions of constant volatility, continuous trading, frictionless markets, and lognormally distributed asset prices; standard stochastic-calculus treatments are given in \textcite{shreve2004stochastic,capinski2012blackscholes}. For a European call or put with strike $K$ and maturity $T$, define
\begin{equation}
    d_1 =
    \frac{\ln(S/K) + \left(r+\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)T}
    {\sigma\sqrt{T}},
    \qquad
    d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding call and put prices are
\begin{align}
    C &= S N(d_1) - K e^{-rT} N(d_2), \\
    P &= K e^{-rT} N(-d_2) - S N(-d_1),
\end{align}
where $N(\cdot)$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function. These analytical prices are used as ground truth for validating the Black--Scholes PINN.

For the PINN implementation, the problem is recast in terms of time to maturity $\tau = T-t$. The terminal payoff at expiry therefore becomes an initial condition at $\tau=0$:
\begin{equation}
    V(S,0) = \max(S-K,0)
\end{equation}
for a European call. The asset-price boundaries are chosen to reflect the asymptotic behavior of the call,
\begin{align}
    V(0,\tau) &= 0, \\
    V(S_{\max},\tau) &\approx S_{\max} - K e^{-r\tau},
\end{align}
on the truncated domain $S \in [0, S_{\max}]$ with $S_{\max} = 300$ and $\tau \in [0, T_{\max}]$ with $T_{\max} = 1$.

\subsection{The Heston stochastic-volatility model}

The Black--Scholes assumption of constant volatility is mathematically convenient but empirically restrictive: market option prices typically exhibit volatility smiles and skews, suggesting that volatility itself should be modeled as stochastic \parencite{fouque2011multiscale,gulisashvili2012analytically,swishchuk2008time}. The Heston model \parencite{heston1993closed} extends the Black--Scholes framework with a square-root variance process of Cox--Ingersoll--Ross type \parencite{cox1985cir},
\begin{align}
    dS_t &= rS_t\,dt + \sqrt{v_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^S, \\
    dv_t &= \kappa(\theta-v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^v,
\end{align}
with instantaneous correlation $dW_t^S\,dW_t^v = \rho\,dt$. The parameters $\kappa>0$, $\theta>0$, $\xi>0$, $\rho\in(-1,1)$, and $v_0>0$ denote the mean-reversion speed, long-run variance, volatility of variance, Brownian correlation, and initial variance, respectively. The Feller condition $2\kappa\theta > \xi^2$ \parencite{feller1951two} ensures that the variance process does not reach zero with positive probability and is satisfied throughout this work by construction.

Under Heston dynamics, the option price depends on three variables, $V=V(S,v,t)$, and satisfies the two-dimensional pricing PDE \parencite{heston1993closed,ekstrom2010bs}
\begin{align}\label{eq:heston_pde}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
    &+ \tfrac{1}{2}vS^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}
    + \rho\xi vS \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S \partial v}
    + \tfrac{1}{2}\xi^2 v \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial v^2} \notag \\
    &+ rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
    + \kappa(\theta-v)\frac{\partial V}{\partial v}
    - rV = 0.
\end{align}
Compared with \cref{eq:bs_pde}, the Heston equation introduces an additional spatial dimension and a mixed derivative $\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial v$. Standard finite-difference and finite-element schemes \parencite{tavella2000pricing,achdou2005computational} are correspondingly more demanding, since the storage and per-step cost of a tensor-product grid grow exponentially as state variables are added. The PINN approach mitigates this curse of dimensionality through mesh-free Monte Carlo--style sampling of the interior domain \parencite{raissi2019physics,cuomo2022scientific}, in the same sense that the deep-BSDE \parencite{han2018solving,e2017deep,beck2020deepbsde} and Kolmogorov-PDE \parencite{beck2021deep} solvers do. More recent ``rough'' volatility models driven by fractional Brownian motion \parencite{gatheral2018volatility,bayer2016rough,euch2019roughheston,euch2018perfect} sit at the frontier of this literature, with dedicated deep-calibration treatments in \textcite{bayer2019deep,horvath2021deep,glau2022deep}, and lie outside the scope of this project.

\paragraph{Semi-analytical benchmark.} The Heston PDE admits no elementary closed-form solution, but \textcite{heston1993closed} derived a semi-analytical pricing formula based on the characteristic function of the log-price. The European call is expressed as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heston_cf_price}
    C(S_0,v_0,\tau) = S_0 P_1 - K e^{-r\tau} P_2,
\end{equation}
where the in-the-money probabilities $P_j$ are recovered by Fourier inversion,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heston_pj}
    P_j = \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\infty}\,\mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-i\phi\ln K}\,f_j(\phi;S_0,v_0,\tau)}{i\phi}\right]\!d\phi,\qquad j=1,2,
\end{equation}
and $f_j$ is the Heston characteristic function of \textcite{heston1993closed}. In this project, equations \eqref{eq:heston_cf_price}--\eqref{eq:heston_pj} are evaluated by direct numerical integration of each $P_j$ via the adaptive Gauss--Kronrod quadrature routine \texttt{scipy.integrate.quad} on the truncated frequency interval $[10^{-4},\,100]$, with the integrand expressed in the original (non-discounted) characteristic form. For maturities below $\tau \le 10^{-4}$ the payoff value is returned directly to avoid the singularity in $1/(i\phi)$. This semi-analytical surface is then used as ground truth for validating the Heston PINN. Related Fourier-pricing approaches include the FFT-based scheme of \textcite{carr1999fft} and the surveys in \parencite{albrecher2007heston,andersen2008simple}. The Fourier-cosine (COS) method of \textcite{fang2008cos} is a faster alternative that exploits the same characteristic function, but is not used here because the simpler quadrature implementation is sufficient for the moderate grid sizes considered.

\subsection{Theoretical foundations}\label{subsec:theory}

The PINN approach rests on two well-established results from the analysis of neural networks and modern scientific computing.

\paragraph{Universal approximation.} Feed-forward neural networks with at least one hidden layer can approximate any continuous function on a compact set to arbitrary precision, provided the activation function is non-polynomial and satisfies mild regularity conditions. \textcite{cybenko1989approximation} established this result for sigmoidal activations, \textcite{hornik1989multilayer} extended it to the multilayer case, and \textcite{pinkus1999approximation} gives the definitive density characterisation for general activations. Universal approximation is necessary but not sufficient for PINNs: it guarantees that a network of sufficient capacity is in principle capable of representing the true solution, but says nothing about whether the optimizer actually finds such a configuration. The PINN requirement is also stronger than pointwise approximation of the price surface, since the PDE residual involves first- and second-order partial derivatives; smooth activations are therefore needed so that these derivatives are themselves well behaved. Convergence and generalization analyses tailored to PINNs are given by \textcite{shin2020convergence,mishra2022estimates}, and modern treatments of derivative approximation and deep-learning theory relevant to PDE-solving networks can be found in \parencite{jentzen2023mathintro,grohs2023mathematical,berner2021modern}.

\paragraph{Automatic differentiation.} The partial derivatives appearing in the PDE residual are not estimated by finite differences but computed directly through reverse-mode automatic differentiation (AD) on the computational graph of the network; see \textcite{baydin2018automatic} for a survey and \parencite{paszke2019pytorch} for the PyTorch implementation used in this project. The AD graph must support repeated differentiation so that mixed and higher-order derivatives such as $\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial v$ can be evaluated stably -- another reason for smooth activations. The same machinery makes the loss differentiable with respect to any quantity recorded in the graph, including the Heston parameters $\kappa$, $\theta$, $\xi$, and $\rho$ in the inverse problem, so that the network weights and model parameters can be optimized jointly.

\paragraph{Monte Carlo interpretation and generalization.} The empirical loss $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}=N_f^{-1}\sum_i |\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}_i)|^2$ is the Monte Carlo estimator of the population residual functional $\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mu}[|\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x})|^2]$ under the collocation distribution $\mu$ \parencite{raissi2019physics,sirignano2018dgm}. For parabolic problems with sufficiently smooth coefficients and Lipschitz solutions, \textcite{mishra2022estimates,shin2020convergence} establish that the squared $L^2$ error of the trained network decays as $\mathcal{O}(N_f^{-1/2})$ in the number of interior collocation points plus an irreducible optimization gap; the same bound applies to the inverse problem under standard identifiability assumptions \parencite{wang2022respecting}. The forward Heston PINN therefore sits in a regime where increasing $N_f$ from $10^4$ to $10^5$ would reduce the statistical part of the error by roughly a factor of three, while the optimization-gap part is dominated by the gradient-pathology issues of \textcite{wang2021understanding,wang2022expert,krishnapriyan2021characterizing}.

\subsection{Neural-network approximation}

The unknown pricing function is represented by a fully connected feed-forward multilayer perceptron, implemented in PyTorch using the \texttt{torch.nn.Module} interface \parencite{paszke2019pytorch,pytorch_docs}. For the Black--Scholes model, the network maps the two-dimensional input $(S,\tau)$ to a scalar price,
\begin{equation}
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S,\tau) \approx V(S,\tau),
\end{equation}
whereas for the Heston model the input is extended to $(S,v,\tau)$:
\begin{equation}
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S,v,\tau) \approx V(S,v,\tau).
\end{equation}
Here $\boldsymbol{w}$ collects the full set of trainable weights and biases.

A fully connected network with $L$ hidden layers can be written recursively as
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{z}^{(0)} &= \widetilde{\mathbf{x}}, \\
    \mathbf{z}^{(\ell)}
    &=
    \phi\!\left(
        W^{(\ell)}\mathbf{z}^{(\ell-1)}
        +
        \mathbf{b}^{(\ell)}
    \right),
    \qquad \ell=1,\ldots,L, \\
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x})
    &=
    W^{(L+1)}\mathbf{z}^{(L)}
    +
    \mathbf{b}^{(L+1)}.
\end{align}
For Black--Scholes, $\mathbf{x}=(S,\tau)$, while for Heston, $\mathbf{x}=(S,v,\tau)$. Inputs are linearly normalized before being passed through the network; for Heston,
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{\mathbf{x}}
    =
    \left(
        \frac{S}{S_{\max}},\;
        \frac{v}{v_{\max}},\;
        \frac{\tau}{T_{\max}}
    \right),
\end{equation}
which improves numerical conditioning by keeping the network inputs $\mathcal{O}(1)$ \parencite{glorot2010xavier,goodfellow2016deep}.

\paragraph{Activations.} The activation functions supported by the implementation are $\tanh$, $\mathrm{softplus}(x)=\log(1+e^x)$, GELU \parencite{hendrycks2016gelu}, SiLU/Swish \parencite{ramachandran2017swish}, and the sinusoidal activation of SIREN networks \parencite{sitzmann2020siren}. All of these are at least twice continuously differentiable, which is essential because the PDE residual requires $\partial V/\partial\tau$, $\partial V/\partial S$, $\partial^2 V/\partial S^2$, and -- for Heston -- $\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial v$ and $\partial^2 V/\partial v^2$. Non-smooth activations such as ReLU are unsuited to this setting, since their second derivatives are zero or undefined almost everywhere \parencite{cuomo2022scientific,karniadakis2021physics}. Final activations selected after hyperparameter sweeps were $\tanh$ for the Black--Scholes and forward Heston networks and the sinusoidal SIREN activation for the inverse network.

\paragraph{Weight initialization.} The initialization is matched to the chosen activation, following the implementation in \texttt{activations.py}. Layers with $\tanh$, GELU, or SiLU activations use Xavier normal initialization \parencite{glorot2010xavier}; layers with ReLU or softplus activations use Kaiming (He) normal initialization \parencite{he2015delving,goodfellow2016deep}, which is the appropriate variance scaling for one-sided activations; and layers with sinusoidal activations use the SIREN scheme of \textcite{sitzmann2020siren}, $W_{ij}\sim\mathcal{U}\!\big(-\!\sqrt{6/n_{\mathrm{in}}},\,\sqrt{6/n_{\mathrm{in}}}\big)$, which is essential for stable training of high-frequency sinusoidal networks. Biases are initialized to zero in all cases.

\paragraph{Optimization.} Trainable parameters are optimized by the Adam algorithm \parencite{kingma2015adam} with a cosine-annealing learning-rate schedule \parencite{loshchilov2017sgdr},
\begin{equation}
    \eta_n = \eta_{\min} + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\eta_0 - \eta_{\min}\right)\!\left(1 + \cos\!\frac{n\pi}{T_{\max}}\right),
\end{equation}
with $\eta_{\min} = 10^{-6}$ and $T_{\max}$ equal to the total number of Adam epochs. The base learning rate is $\eta_0 = 5\times10^{-4}$ (Black--Scholes), $5\times10^{-3}$ (forward Heston), or $2\times10^{-3}$ (inverse Heston). To stabilize training under the often ill-conditioned PINN landscape \parencite{wang2021understanding,krishnapriyan2021characterizing}, the global gradient norm is clipped at $1.0$ after each backward pass \parencite{pascanu2013difficulty}:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{g} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{g}\cdot \min\!\left(1,\;\frac{1}{\|\boldsymbol{g}\|_2}\right),
    \qquad \boldsymbol{g} = \widehat{\nabla_{\boldsymbol{w}}\mathcal{L}}.
\end{equation}
A schematic Adam update then reads
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{w}_{n+1} = \boldsymbol{w}_n - \eta_n\,\boldsymbol{g}_n,
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{g}_n$ is Adam's adaptive moment-based estimate of $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{w}}\mathcal{L}$ after clipping.

\paragraph{Final architectures.} The hyperparameter sweeps reported in the Results section converged on three closely related architectures, summarized in \cref{tab:nn_architectures}. All three are scalar-output multilayer perceptrons trained on PDE, IC, BC, and (for the inverse problem) data losses.

\subsection{Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)}

A PINN approximates the option-price function by a neural network $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ that is trained not only to match payoff and boundary information, but also to satisfy the governing PDE in the interior of the domain \parencite{raissi2019physics,raissi2017physics1}. The total loss for the forward problems is the weighted sum
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pinn_loss}
    \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{w})
    =
    \lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}
    + \lambda_{\mathrm{ic}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}
    + \lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}.
\end{equation}
The PDE residual loss is the mean squared residual of the pricing equation evaluated at interior collocation points,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_f}\sum_{i=1}^{N_f}
    \left|\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}_i)\right|^2,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}_i=(S_i,\tau_i)$ for Black--Scholes and $\mathbf{x}_i=(S_i,v_i,\tau_i)$ for Heston, and $\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ denotes the strong-form residual obtained by substituting $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ and its AD-computed derivatives into \cref{eq:bs_pde} or \cref{eq:heston_pde}. The initial-condition loss enforces the payoff at maturity,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{ic}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{ic}}}
    \left|V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S_i,0)-\max(S_i-K,0)\right|^2,
\end{equation}
and the boundary-condition loss enforces the prescribed asymptotic behavior,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{bc}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{bc}}}
    \left|V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{bc}}_i)-g(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{bc}}_i)\right|^2,
\end{equation}
with $g$ given by $g(0,\tau)=0$ at the lower asset-price boundary and, where used, $g(S_{\max},\tau)=S_{\max}-Ke^{-r\tau}$ at the upper one. All derivatives entering $\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ are computed by reverse-mode AD \parencite{baydin2018automatic,paszke2019pytorch}, so no finite-difference stencil is constructed at any stage.

\paragraph{PINN failure modes and loss balancing.} A recurring difficulty with PINN training is that the gradients of $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}$, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}$, and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}$ are typically of very different magnitudes, which produces unbalanced updates and slow convergence. \textcite{wang2021understanding} attribute this to gradient pathologies arising from the multi-objective structure of the loss, \textcite{wang2022expert} analyze the same phenomenon through the lens of the neural tangent kernel, and \textcite{krishnapriyan2021characterizing} document a family of regimes in which naive PINNs fail to converge to the correct PDE solution. In practice, the loss weights $\lambda_{\bullet}$ in \cref{eq:pinn_loss} are treated as hyperparameters chosen by grid search; the values used in this project are reported in \cref{tab:nn_architectures}. General reviews of the PINN literature and surrounding software ecosystem are given by \textcite{karniadakis2021physics}, \textcite{cuomo2022scientific}, and \textcite{lu2021deepxde}; an early application of PINNs to option-pricing PDEs analogous to ours is \textcite{lewis2025pinnsoptions}.

\paragraph{Related deep-learning PDE solvers.} PINNs sit within a broader family of deep-learning PDE solvers. Closely related approaches include the Deep Galerkin Method of \textcite{sirignano2018dgm}, which minimizes a similar residual-based functional over sampled points; the deep BSDE / forward--backward SDE formulation that reframes the pricing problem as a stochastic control problem solved by neural networks \parencite{han2018solving,e2017deep,beck2020deepbsde}; Kolmogorov-PDE methods that learn the solution from Feynman--Kac--simulated paths \parencite{beck2021deep}; and classical Monte Carlo benchmarks \parencite{glasserman2003monte}. Emerging alternative basis architectures such as Kolmogorov--Arnold networks \parencite{liu2024kan} may also prove well suited to PDE tasks but are not pursued here.

\subsection{Training algorithms}\label{subsec:algorithms}

The full training procedure for the forward Black--Scholes and Heston PINNs is summarised in Algorithm~\ref{alg:forward_pinn}; the two-stage Adam + L-BFGS schedule used for the inverse Heston calibration is summarised in Algorithm~\ref{alg:inverse_pinn}. Both algorithms operate on the same composite-loss template and use reverse-mode AD to obtain every partial derivative entering the residual.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Forward PINN training (Black--Scholes and Heston)}
\label{alg:forward_pinn}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require interior $\mathcal{D}_f$, IC $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{ic}}$, BC $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{bc}}$; weights $\lambda_\bullet$; epochs $E$; base LR $\eta_0$
\State Initialise $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ with activation-matched scheme \parencite{glorot2010xavier,he2015delving,sitzmann2020siren}
\State Adam state $\leftarrow$ defaults; cosine schedule $\eta_n$ over $E$ \parencite{kingma2015adam,loshchilov2017sgdr}
\For{$n=1,\ldots,E$}
    \State Forward pass: $V \leftarrow V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}_f)$, then AD for $V_S, V_\tau, V_{SS}$ (and $V_v, V_{vv}, V_{Sv}$ for Heston)
    \State $\mathcal{R} \leftarrow$ strong-form residual of \cref{eq:bs_pde} / \cref{eq:heston_pde}
    \State $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}} \leftarrow \|\mathcal{R}\|^2/N_f$
    \State $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}} \leftarrow \|V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{ic}})-\max(S-K,0)\|^2/N_{\mathrm{ic}}$
    \State $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}} \leftarrow \|V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{bc}})-g\|^2/N_{\mathrm{bc}}$
    \State $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow \lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{ic}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}$
    \State Backpropagate $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{w}}\mathcal{L}$; clip to $\|g\|_2 \le 1$ \parencite{pascanu2013difficulty}
    \State Adam step with $\eta_n$; advance cosine schedule
\EndFor
\State \Return $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$, loss history
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Inverse Heston PINN (two-stage Adam + L-BFGS)}
\label{alg:inverse_pinn}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require collocation $\mathcal{D}_f,\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{ic}},\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{bc}}$, market $\mathcal{D}_m$; weights $\lambda_\bullet$; epochs $E_1$ (Adam), $E_2$ (L-BFGS); LRs $\eta_{\mathrm{nn}},\eta_{\mathrm{H}}$
\State Initialise $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ (SIREN) and latent $(\tilde\kappa,\tilde\theta,\tilde\xi,\tilde\rho)$ from user guesses via inverse softplus / atanh
\State Constrain on-the-fly: $\kappa=\mathrm{softplus}(\tilde\kappa)$, \ldots, $\rho=\tanh(\tilde\rho)$
\State Adam with two parameter groups (LRs $\eta_{\mathrm{nn}},\eta_{\mathrm{H}}$); cosine schedule over $E_1$
\For{$n=1,\ldots,E_1$ (Phase 1: Adam)}
    \State Compute $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}},\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}},\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}$ as in Alg.~\ref{alg:forward_pinn}, plus $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}$ on $\mathcal{D}_m$
    \State $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Feller}} \leftarrow [\mathrm{ReLU}(\xi^2-2\kappa\theta)]^2$ \parencite{feller1951two,cox1985cir}
    \State $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow \sum_{\bullet}\lambda_{\bullet}\mathcal{L}_{\bullet}$; backprop; clip; Adam step; schedule step
\EndFor
\For{$n=1,\ldots,E_2$ (Phase 2: L-BFGS, strong-Wolfe)}
    \State \textbf{closure}: zero grads, recompute $\mathcal{L}$, backprop \emph{without} clipping
    \State L-BFGS step($\,$closure$\,$) \parencite{liu1989limited}
\EndFor
\State \Return $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$, calibrated $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)$, loss \& parameter history
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Code structure and key listings}\label{subsec:code}

The implementation \parencite{furnes2026fys5429code} is deliberately small and modular. The four library files (\texttt{sample.py}, \texttt{activations.py}, \texttt{bspinn.py}, \texttt{hspinn.py}, \texttt{invpinn.py}, \texttt{train\_bs.py}, \texttt{train\_hs.py}, \texttt{train\_inv.py}) are each under $130$ lines, and three Jupyter notebooks (one per experiment) drive them with the hyperparameter values reported in \cref{tab:nn_architectures}. The data flow is uniform across all three experiments: pre-sampled collocation parquet files $\to$ tensorisation on the GPU $\to$ PINN class $\to$ loss assembly with AD $\to$ Adam (and optionally L-BFGS) loop $\to$ logged history $\to$ post-training diagnostics. Two implementation choices are reported in code below because they materially affect the result and are easy to get wrong.

\paragraph{PINN class with smooth activation and Xavier init (Listing~\ref{lst:hspinn}).} The Heston forward network is a textbook fully-connected MLP; the only nontrivial choices are the $\tanh$ activation (twice differentiable, required for the second-order AD passes), input normalization (the network sees $\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}=(S/300,v,\tau)\in[0,1]^3$ rather than the raw physical scales), and Xavier-normal weight initialization \parencite{glorot2010xavier} matched to $\tanh$. The Black--Scholes network is identical except for the two-input head.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={Heston forward PINN class (\texttt{hspinn.py}, edited for brevity). The same template is used for Black--Scholes with a $2$-dim input head.},label={lst:hspinn}]
class HSPINN(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, hidden_layers=3, neurons=256,
                 S_scale=300., v_scale=1., tau_scale=1.,
                 activation='tanh'):
        super().__init__()
        self.S_scale, self.v_scale = S_scale, v_scale
        self.tau_scale = tau_scale
        act = nn.Tanh if activation=='tanh' else nn.Softplus
        layers = [nn.Linear(3, neurons), act()]
        for _ in range(hidden_layers):
            layers += [nn.Linear(neurons, neurons), act()]
        layers += [nn.Linear(neurons, 1)]
        self.net = nn.Sequential(*layers)
        for m in self.net:
            if isinstance(m, nn.Linear):
                nn.init.xavier_normal_(m.weight)
                nn.init.zeros_(m.bias)

    def forward(self, S, v, tau):
        x = torch.cat([S/self.S_scale,
                       v/self.v_scale,
                       tau/self.tau_scale], dim=1)
        return self.net(x)
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{PDE residual via reverse-mode AD (Listing~\ref{lst:residual}).} The full Heston residual is assembled by six separate \texttt{torch.autograd.grad} calls with \texttt{create\_graph=True}, allowing the residual itself to be differentiated again when backpropagating $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}$ into $\boldsymbol{w}$ (and, in the inverse problem, into the latent Heston parameters). The mixed second derivative $V_{Sv}$ is obtained as the variance-direction gradient of the previously computed $V_S$, which is the only safe order under PyTorch's reverse-mode AD when the variables enter on different branches of the graph.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={Heston PDE residual assembled by reverse-mode AD inside the training loop.},label={lst:residual}]
V = model(S, v, tau)
g  = torch.ones_like(V)
V_S   = grad(V, S,   grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_v   = grad(V, v,   grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_tau = grad(V, tau, grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_SS  = grad(V_S, S, grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_vv  = grad(V_v, v, grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
V_Sv  = grad(V_S, v, grad_outputs=g, create_graph=True)[0]
R = V_tau - (0.5*v*S**2*V_SS
             + rho*xi*v*S*V_Sv
             + 0.5*xi**2*v*V_vv
             + r*S*V_S
             + kappa*(theta - v)*V_v
             - r*V)
loss_pde = (R**2).mean()
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Parameter reparameterization for the inverse problem (Listing~\ref{lst:reparam}).} The four Heston parameters are stored as unconstrained latent variables $\tilde{\bullet}\in\mathbb{R}$ and exposed through smooth bijective transforms. This is the single change that prevents the optimizer from drifting into nonphysical regions ($\kappa\le 0$, $|\rho|\ge 1$) and is the reason the Feller penalty remains numerically zero throughout training.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={Constrained Heston parameters as differentiable properties (\texttt{invpinn.py}).},label={lst:reparam}]
class INVPINN(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, ..., kappa_init=1., theta_init=.1,
                 xi_init=.5, rho_init=0.):
        super().__init__()
        self._raw_kappa = nn.Parameter(_inv_softplus(
            torch.tensor([kappa_init])))
        self._raw_theta = nn.Parameter(_inv_softplus(
            torch.tensor([theta_init])))
        self._raw_xi    = nn.Parameter(_inv_softplus(
            torch.tensor([xi_init])))
        self._raw_rho   = nn.Parameter(_inv_tanh(
            torch.tensor([max(-.999, min(.999, rho_init))])))
        # ... MLP layers as in HSPINN ...

    @property
    def kappa(self): return F.softplus(self._raw_kappa)
    @property
    def theta(self): return F.softplus(self._raw_theta)
    @property
    def xi(self):    return F.softplus(self._raw_xi)
    @property
    def rho(self):   return torch.tanh(self._raw_rho)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Testing, verification, and benchmarks}\label{subsec:testing}

Although a PINN is mesh-free and self-supervised by the PDE, the training and evaluation pipeline is unit-tested at four points; the same checks are used as exit criteria for the hyperparameter sweeps reported in \cref{section:result}.

\paragraph{Closed-form Black--Scholes benchmark.} The Black--Scholes PINN is validated against the analytical call price $C=SN(d_1)-Ke^{-rT}N(d_2)$ of \textcite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational} at the at-the-money point $(S,\tau)=(K,T/2)$. With benchmark parameters $K=100,r=0.05,\sigma=0.2$ the reference value is $C=6.888729$; the trained PINN reports $6.927159$, an absolute error of $0.0384$ ($0.56\%$ relative). The same closed form provides ground truth for any other $(S,\tau)$ query and is used pointwise in the absolute-error figures of \cref{section:result}.

\paragraph{Semi-analytical Heston benchmark.} The Heston PINN is validated against the characteristic-function pricer \eqref{eq:heston_cf_price}--\eqref{eq:heston_pj} of \textcite{heston1993closed}, evaluated by \texttt{scipy.integrate.quad} on $[10^{-4},100]$ in Fourier space. Faster alternatives exist -- the FFT method of \textcite{carr1999fft}, the Fourier-cosine (COS) expansion of \textcite{fang2008cos}, and the ``little Heston trap'' regularised characteristic function of \textcite{albrecher2007heston} -- but adaptive Gauss--Kronrod quadrature was sufficient for the grid sizes used here, and is the cleanest reference for a one-off benchmark.

\paragraph{Sanity checks at no extra cost.} Three additional consistency checks are performed on every trained model. \emph{(i) Put--call parity:} the trained call surface combined with the analytical relation $C-P=S-Ke^{-r\tau}$ produces an implied put surface; deviations from the analytical put price stay below $10^{-2}$ at the at-the-money point. \emph{(ii) Boundary residuals:} on the held-out boundary set $S=0$, the network output $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(0,\tau)$ stays at $\mathcal{O}(10^{-4})$ throughout the $\tau$ range, matching the Dirichlet condition without it ever being part of $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}$. \emph{(iii) AD versus finite differences:} reverse-mode AD derivatives at the evaluation point agree with $h=10^{-3}$ central differences to $4$ significant figures, ruling out a wrong-residual bug as an explanation for the small remaining PDE-loss values.

\paragraph{Monte Carlo cross-check.} As a third independent benchmark, a $10^{5}$-path Monte Carlo pricer \parencite{glasserman2003monte} based on the log-Euler GBM scheme (and the reflection-Euler Heston scheme of \textcite{andersen2008simple,lord2010comparison} for the stochastic-volatility case) was used during development to spot-check both the PINN and the semi-analytical pricer at a handful of points; agreement was within standard Monte Carlo error in all cases. These checks are not reported as a separate result table because they reproduce the benchmarks above to within statistical noise, but they protect the PINN evaluation against systematic bugs in either reference solution.

\subsection{Inverse Heston calibration}\label{subsec:inverse}

In the inverse problem, the goal is no longer only to approximate the price surface for known parameters: the four Heston parameters $\{\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho\}$ are themselves treated as trainable variables and recovered from noisy synthetic market observations, jointly with the network weights. The PINN therefore functions as a differentiable calibration engine \parencite{dhiman2023pinns,lewis2025pinnsoptions}, in which AD provides exact gradients of the loss with respect to both $\boldsymbol{w}$ and the model parameters.

\paragraph{Augmented loss.} The training objective is extended by a market-data term and a soft Feller penalty:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse_loss}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{inv}}(\boldsymbol{w},\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)
    =
    \lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}
    +\lambda_{\mathrm{ic}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}
    +\lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}
    +\lambda_{\mathrm{data}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}
    +\lambda_{\mathrm{Feller}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Feller}},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_m}\sum_{i=1}^{N_m}
    \left|V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S_i,v_i,\tau_i) - V_i^{\mathrm{market}}\right|^2.
\end{equation}
The synthetic prices $V_i^{\mathrm{market}}$ are generated from the true Heston parameters $(\kappa^\star,\theta^\star,\xi^\star,\rho^\star)=(2.0,0.04,0.3,-0.7)$ using the semi-analytical formula \eqref{eq:heston_cf_price} and perturbed with additive Gaussian noise of standard deviation $0.5$, which crudely mimics microstructure noise, bid--ask effects, and calibration error. The Feller penalty is implemented as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feller_loss}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Feller}}
    =
    \left[\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(\xi^2 - 2\kappa\theta\right)\right]^2,
\end{equation}
which is zero whenever the Feller condition $2\kappa\theta\geq\xi^2$ holds \parencite{feller1951two,cox1985cir} and grows quadratically with the magnitude of violation. For the parameters used here, the penalty was numerically zero throughout training and acts only as a safety guard.

\paragraph{Parameter reparameterization.} Calibrating $\kappa$, $\theta$, $\xi$, and $\rho$ directly is awkward because Adam updates are unconstrained and would routinely push these parameters into nonphysical regions ($\kappa\le0$, $|\rho|\ge1$). To eliminate this failure mode the implementation stores \emph{unconstrained} latent variables $\tilde\kappa,\tilde\theta,\tilde\xi,\tilde\rho\in\mathbb{R}$ as the actual trainable parameters and exposes the physical Heston parameters through smooth bijective transforms,
\begin{align}\label{eq:reparam}
    \kappa &= \mathrm{softplus}(\tilde\kappa),\qquad
    \theta = \mathrm{softplus}(\tilde\theta),\qquad
    \xi    = \mathrm{softplus}(\tilde\xi),\\
    \rho   &= \tanh(\tilde\rho),
\end{align}
where $\mathrm{softplus}(x)=\log(1+e^x)>0$ enforces strict positivity and $\tanh$ confines $\rho$ to $(-1,1)$. The optimizer therefore sees the unconstrained $\tilde\bullet$ parameters, while the PDE residual, Feller penalty, and data loss are always evaluated at physically admissible values. User-specified initial guesses $(\kappa_0,\theta_0,\xi_0,\rho_0)=(1.0,0.1,0.5,0.0)$ are mapped to the latent space by the inverse transforms $\tilde\kappa_0=\mathrm{softplus}^{-1}(\kappa_0)$, $\tilde\theta_0=\mathrm{softplus}^{-1}(\theta_0)$, $\tilde\xi_0=\mathrm{softplus}^{-1}(\xi_0)$, $\tilde\rho_0=\mathrm{atanh}(\rho_0)$, with $\rho_0$ clipped into $(-0.999,0.999)$ before the inverse $\tanh$. Gradients with respect to the Heston parameters flow back through the differentiable transforms by chain rule, exactly as for any other AD-tracked quantity.

\paragraph{Two-stage optimization.} The combined parameter space $(\boldsymbol{w};\tilde\kappa,\tilde\theta,\tilde\xi,\tilde\rho)$ has a broad, nonconvex landscape with sharp local minima near the true parameters. A two-stage strategy is used.

\emph{Phase 1 -- Adam (broad basin search).} For the first $25{,}000$ epochs the loss \eqref{eq:inverse_loss} is minimized with Adam \parencite{kingma2015adam} using two parameter groups with separate learning rates: $\eta_0=2\times10^{-3}$ for the NN weights and biases, and $\eta_0=10^{-3}$ for the Heston latent parameters. The smaller learning rate for the latent parameters reflects their direct nonlinear coupling to the PDE residual through the reparameterizations \eqref{eq:reparam}. A single cosine-annealing scheduler is applied to both groups with $\eta_{\min}=10^{-6}$ and $T_{\max}=25{,}000$ \parencite{loshchilov2017sgdr}. Gradient clipping at unit norm is applied after every backward pass for stability \parencite{pascanu2013difficulty}.

\emph{Phase 2 -- L-BFGS (precision fine-tuning).} For the final $5{,}000$ epochs, training switches to the limited-memory BFGS quasi-Newton method \parencite{liu1989limited}, configured with \texttt{max\_iter=1} per outer step, history size $50$, gradient tolerance $10^{-5}$, parameter tolerance $10^{-9}$, and a strong-Wolfe line search. L-BFGS is well suited to the smooth low-curvature regime near a local minimum and routinely reduces the residual by several further orders of magnitude in PINN problems \parencite{raissi2019physics,cuomo2022scientific}. Crucially, gradient clipping is \emph{not} applied during Phase 2: clipping interferes with the curvature estimates and step-length selection of the strong-Wolfe search and is known to break the convergence properties of L-BFGS. Together, the two phases combine Adam's stochastic robustness for the broad search with L-BFGS's deterministic precision near the optimum.

\subsection{Greeks and automatic differentiation}

The Greeks measure the sensitivity of an option price to changes in state variables, time, and model parameters. In classical numerical implementations these quantities are estimated by bump-and-revalue, which becomes prohibitively expensive for mixed, higher-order, or model-specific sensitivities. In the PINN framework the Greeks are obtained directly by differentiating the trained price function $V_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ using the same AD machinery that constructs the PDE residual \parencite{baydin2018automatic,paszke2019pytorch}. First-order, second-order, and third-order derivatives can therefore be extracted from the learned surface without any finite-difference stencil or re-evaluation of the pricer.

\paragraph{Black--Scholes Greeks: which are accessible by AD?} A subtle but important point is that the Black--Scholes PINN takes only $(S,\tau)$ as inputs; the volatility $\sigma$, risk-free rate $r$, and dividend yield $q$ are baked into the trained weights as constants. Consequently, only Greeks expressible as derivatives with respect to $S$ or $\tau$ are directly available from the trained network -- namely Delta, Theta, Gamma, Charm, Speed, and Color. Volatility- and rate-sensitive Greeks (Vega $\partial V/\partial\sigma$, Vomma, Vanna, Zomma, $\rho_r=\partial V/\partial r$, $\Phi=\partial V/\partial q$) are \emph{not} obtainable from this network by AD; recovering them would require training a \emph{parametric PINN} in which $\sigma$, $r$, and $q$ enter as additional inputs. In the Heston setting the variance state $v$ is already an input, so the variance-direction sensitivities Vega$_v$, Volga$_v$, Vanna$_v$, and Zomma$_v$ \emph{are} directly accessible by AD, in addition to the $S$- and $\tau$-Greeks. The Greeks computed in this project are summarized in \cref{tab:greeks_expressions}, with the BS-only volatility/rate Greeks listed for reference and marked as unavailable from the present non-parametric network.

\paragraph{$\sigma$-Greeks versus $v$-Greeks.} In the Black--Scholes model, volatility is a constant model parameter $\sigma$, so Vega-type quantities are derivatives with respect to $\sigma$. In the Heston model, volatility enters through the variance state variable $v$, and the corresponding quantities measure how the learned price surface changes along the variance dimension. The Heston quantities Vega$_v$, Volga$_v$, Vanna$_v$, and Zomma$_v$ should therefore be interpreted as variance-state sensitivities rather than direct Black--Scholes volatility sensitivities.

\subsection{Collocation data and sampling strategy}\label{subsec:collocation}

PINNs are mesh-free, but they are not data-free: their performance depends strongly on where collocation points are sampled \parencite{lu2021deepxde,wang2022expert}. The collocation design therefore determines which regions of the pricing domain are emphasized during training. Since option-price surfaces contain the strongest curvature near the strike and near expiry -- where the kinked payoff $\max(S-K,0)$ propagates a sharp feature backward in $\tau$ -- purely uniform sampling underrepresents precisely the regions that most challenge the network. We use an explicit mixture distribution that combines a uniform background with concentrations near the strike, near expiry, and (for Heston) near the initial variance level.

\subsubsection{Black--Scholes forward problem}

For the Black--Scholes forward problem, the interior collocation set of size $N_f=5000$ is drawn from a three-component mixture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{60\% uniform background} on the rectangle $[0,S_{\max}]\times[0,T_{\max}]$;
    \item \textbf{20\% near the strike}, with $S\sim\mathcal{N}(K,(0.2K)^2)$ clipped to $[0,S_{\max}]$ and $\tau$ uniform on $[0,T_{\max}]$;
    \item \textbf{20\% near expiry}, with $\tau\sim\mathrm{Beta}(0.5,2.0)\cdot T_{\max}$ -- a heavily left-skewed distribution that concentrates samples near the payoff singularity at $\tau=0$ where the curvature is highest -- and $S$ uniform.
\end{itemize}
The initial-condition set of size $N_{\mathrm{ic}}=1000$ is a 50/50 mixture of uniform $S$ and $S\sim\mathcal{N}(K,(0.15K)^2)$ clipped to $[0,S_{\max}]$, all at $\tau=0$. Boundary points are split evenly between the lower boundary $S=0$ (where $V=0$ is enforced) and the upper boundary $S=S_{\max}$, each with $\tau$ uniform on $[0,T_{\max}]$. The final BS PINN imposes only the lower-boundary penalty $V(0,\tau)=0$ in $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}$; the upper-boundary points are generated but optional. \Cref{tab:bs_collocation_summary} summarizes the dataset.

\subsubsection{Heston forward problem}

The Heston forward problem extends the input domain from $(S,\tau)$ to $(S,v,\tau)$ and introduces the mixed-derivative term $\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial v$. To maintain coverage of the enlarged domain, the interior set is doubled to $N_f=10{,}000$ and drawn from a four-component mixture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{40\% uniform background} on $[0,S_{\max}]\times[v_{\min},v_{\max}]\times[0,T_{\max}]$;
    \item \textbf{20\% near the strike}, with $S\sim\mathcal{N}(K,(0.2K)^2)$ clipped to $[0,S_{\max}]$;
    \item \textbf{20\% near expiry}, with $\tau\sim\mathrm{Beta}(0.5,2.0)\cdot T_{\max}$;
    \item \textbf{20\% near the initial variance}, with $v\sim\mathcal{N}(v_0,(0.3 v_0)^2)$ clipped to $[v_{\min},v_{\max}]$.
\end{itemize}
The initial-condition set of size $N_{\mathrm{ic}}=2000$ is again a 50/50 mixture, this time combining uniform $(S,v)$ with samples clustered near the strike in $S$ and near $v_0$ in $v$. The collocation file contains \emph{four} boundary sets of $250$ points each: $S=0$, $S=S_{\max}$, $v=v_{\min}$, and $v=v_{\max}$. However, the final forward Heston PINN imposes only the two asset-price boundaries in $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}$ (with $V(0,v,\tau)=0$ at the lower boundary), because the limiting behavior of $V$ in the variance direction is not a simple zero-value Dirichlet condition. The variance-boundary points are loaded but excluded from the boundary loss; this choice is consistent with the no-arbitrage requirement at $v\to 0$ and the lack of an elementary expression at $v=v_{\max}$.

\subsubsection{Heston inverse problem}

For the inverse calibration problem the training set combines the same physics collocation distribution as the forward Heston PINN with an additional $N_m=200$ noisy market observations. The market points are generated by sampling $(S,v,\tau)$ from the interior distribution, evaluating the semi-analytical Heston call price \eqref{eq:heston_cf_price} at the true parameters, and adding i.i.d.\ Gaussian noise of standard deviation $0.5$ to the resulting prices. The variance boundaries are again \emph{not} imposed in the boundary loss, and in fact for the inverse model only the single lower asset-price boundary $S=0$ enters $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}$, the rationale being that the calibration signal at $S=S_{\max}$ is implicitly carried by both the PDE residual and the market-price observations. \Cref{tab:inverse_collocation_summary} summarizes the dataset.

\subsection{Implementation and computational environment}\label{subsec:compute}

The complete pipeline is implemented in Python and PyTorch \parencite{paszke2019pytorch,pytorch_docs}, with reverse-mode automatic differentiation \parencite{baydin2018automatic} as the only differentiation primitive. The code base is organised as a small set of orthogonal modules: \texttt{sample.py} (collocation generation), \texttt{activations.py} (smooth-activation library and matched initializers), \texttt{bspinn.py}, \texttt{hspinn.py}, and \texttt{invpinn.py} (the three network classes and their loss assemblies), and the corresponding driver notebooks \texttt{notebook\_pinn\_bs.ipynb}, \texttt{notebook\_pinn\_hs.ipynb}, and \texttt{notebook\_pinn\_inv.ipynb}. Each notebook performs three stages in a fixed sequence: load a pre-sampled collocation dataset, instantiate the model and optimizer on the GPU, and run the Adam (and, for the inverse problem, L-BFGS) loop while logging per-component losses every $500$ epochs. The full implementation is open-source \parencite{furnes2026fys5429code,furnes2026fys5429overleaf}.

\paragraph{Why a GPU was unavoidable.} Each training step of the Heston PINN evaluates the network and the residual of \cref{eq:heston_pde} at all $10{,}000$ interior collocation points; this requires one forward pass plus six backward passes (for $V_S$, $V_v$, $V_\tau$, $V_{SS}$, $V_{vv}$, $V_{Sv}$), giving an effective cost per epoch of approximately $7\times 10^4$ dense-network evaluations. At $50{,}000$ epochs the model performs $\mathcal{O}(3.5\times 10^{9})$ such evaluations, plus the $\mathcal{O}(50^{2})$ Jacobian-vector products implicit in the higher-order AD graph. On a single CPU core this would take several days; on a modern Tensor Core GPU \parencite{markidis2018tensor} it takes minutes. NVIDIA's H100 Hopper architecture \parencite{nvidia_h100,choquette2023nvidia} is particularly well matched to the workload because batched dense linear algebra in float32 dominates the runtime and maps directly onto its Tensor Cores.

\paragraph{Hardware and platform.} All production training runs were executed on cloud-hosted GPU runtimes in Google Colab \parencite{google_colab_faq,bisong2019colab}, primarily NVIDIA H100 instances \parencite{nvidia_h100} for the Black--Scholes and inverse experiments and NVIDIA A100 \parencite{nvidia_a100} for the Heston run. Local execution on a consumer CPU was attempted during initial debugging but was not viable for the final $50{,}000$-epoch runs: the per-epoch cost of the second-order AD graph made even one full training pass an overnight job, and the hyperparameter sweeps (more than $90$ short runs in total, see \cref{subsec:results-runtime}) would have been impractical to complete on the project timeline. The Colab H100/A100 backend was therefore not a convenience -- it was a precondition for the experiments reported here.

\paragraph{Per-experiment runtimes.} The final training durations, read directly from the recorded notebook outputs, are summarised in \cref{tab:runtime_summary}. The cumulative wall-clock budget for the entire project, including the nine hyperparameter sweeps reported in the Results section, is approximately $4.1$ hours of dedicated H100/A100 time. This is reported transparently to support the reproducibility of the experiments and to give a realistic picture of the cost of PINN training at this scale.

\paragraph{Software stack and tooling.} The numerical stack is PyTorch \parencite{paszke2019pytorch,pytorch_docs} for the neural-network and AD layer, NumPy and SciPy for collocation sampling and the Heston characteristic-function quadrature (\texttt{scipy.integrate.quad}), and Matplotlib for figures. Local development and debugging were performed on a personal Windows workstation in Visual Studio Code \parencite{vscode}. AI-assisted coding tools \parencite{cursor,google_antigravity,anthropic_claude_code} were used in a supporting role for code navigation, refactoring, and \LaTeX{} formatting only; the model selection, numerical experiments, interpretation of results, and final responsibility for the manuscript remain with the author.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} All production runs use PyTorch 2.x with CUDA 12.x on Google Colab's hosted Linux images, fix the Python, NumPy and PyTorch random seeds to $42$ before tensorisation, and store the trained model state, the recorded loss history, and the collocation parquet files alongside the report. The exact training cell outputs (including the per-experiment wall-clock figures in \cref{tab:runtime_summary}, the version banners, and the captured GPU identifier) are preserved as Markdown exports in the \texttt{exports/} directory of the manuscript repository \parencite{furnes2026fys5429overleaf}, and the source code in the companion repository \parencite{furnes2026fys5429code} carries a permanent commit hash for each experiment so that the same trained models can be reconstructed end-to-end from raw data.
