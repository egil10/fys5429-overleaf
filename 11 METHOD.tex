\section{Methods}\label{section:methods}

This section describes the mathematical models, neural-network formulation, automatic-differentiation procedure, and collocation strategies used in the project. The overall objective is to approximate option-price surfaces by training PINNs that satisfy the governing pricing PDEs while also respecting terminal payoff and boundary conditions. 

The Black--Scholes model is first used as a controlled benchmark with an analytical solution, before extending the same framework to the higher-dimensional Heston stochastic-volatility model and finally to an inverse calibration problem.

\subsection{Notation and conventions}\label{subsec:notation}

Since the project moves between two financial models and a neural-network parameterization, several Greek letters carry context-dependent meanings. To avoid ambiguity, the following conventions are used throughout. The neural-network weights and biases are collected into a single parameter vector $\boldsymbol{w}$, written in boldface, so that the symbol $\theta$ is reserved exclusively for the Heston long-run variance. Likewise, the Black--Scholes rate sensitivity (Rho) is denoted $\rho_r$ to distinguish it from the Heston Brownian correlation $\rho$. Loss-function components carry subscripts identifying their role (PDE residual, initial condition, boundary condition, market data), and each is associated with a non-negative weight $\lambda_{\bullet}$. Collocation-point counts use the symbol $N$ with a subscript indicating the corresponding loss term. The PDE residual operator acting on the network output is denoted $\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}$. These conventions are summarized in \cref{tab:notation}.

\subsection{From option-pricing PDEs to diffusion equations}

The connection between option pricing and physics arises from the diffusion structure of the Black--Scholes equation. Under the standard geometric Brownian motion model,
\begin{equation}
    dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t,
\end{equation}
the value $V(S,t)$ of a European option satisfies, under risk-neutral pricing, the Black--Scholes PDE \parencite{profeta2010option}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bs_pde}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
    + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}
    + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
    - rV = 0.
\end{equation}
Here $S$ denotes the underlying asset price, $t$ calendar time, $r$ the risk-free interest rate, and $\sigma$ the volatility.

A key analytical insight is that \cref{eq:bs_pde} can be transformed into a standard diffusion equation \parencite{capinski2012blackscholes}. Introducing the log-moneyness coordinate and time-to-maturity variable
\begin{equation}
    x = \ln\left(\frac{S}{K}\right),
    \qquad
    \tau = T-t,
\end{equation}
together with a suitable exponential rescaling of the dependent variable, reduces the Black--Scholes equation to the canonical heat equation
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau}
    =
    \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}.
\end{equation}
This transformation motivates the use of PINNs: instead of solving the pricing PDE on a fixed mesh, the neural network is trained to learn a smooth function whose derivatives satisfy the same diffusion-type structure at sampled collocation points.

\subsection{The Black--Scholes model}

The Black--Scholes model \parencite{black1973pricing} provides a closed-form benchmark for European option prices under the assumptions of constant volatility, continuous trading, frictionless markets, and lognormally distributed asset prices. For a European call or put with strike $K$ and maturity $T$, define
\begin{equation}
    d_1 =
    \frac{\ln(S/K) + \left(r+\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)T}
    {\sigma\sqrt{T}},
    \qquad
    d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding call and put prices are
\begin{align}
    C &= S N(d_1) - K e^{-rT} N(d_2), \\
    P &= K e^{-rT} N(-d_2) - S N(-d_1),
\end{align}
where $N(\cdot)$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function. These analytical prices are used as ground truth for validating the Black--Scholes PINN.

For the PINN implementation, the problem is expressed in terms of time to maturity $\tau = T-t$. The terminal payoff at expiry therefore becomes an initial condition at $\tau=0$:
\begin{equation}
    V(S,0) = \max(S-K,0)
\end{equation}
for a European call option. The lower and upper asset-price boundaries are chosen to reflect the asymptotic behavior of the call price:
\begin{align}
    V(0,\tau) &\approx 0, \\
    V(S_{\max},\tau) &\approx S_{\max} - K e^{-r\tau}.
\end{align}

\subsection{The Heston stochastic-volatility model}

The Black--Scholes assumption of constant volatility is mathematically convenient but empirically restrictive. Market option prices typically imply volatility smiles and skews, suggesting that volatility itself should be treated as stochastic \parencite{fouque2011multiscale,gulisashvili2012analytically,swishchuk2008time}. The Heston model \parencite{heston1993closed} extends Black--Scholes by introducing a stochastic variance process $v_t$:
\begin{align}
    dS_t &= rS_t\,dt + \sqrt{v_t}S_t\,dW_t^S, \\
    dv_t &= \kappa(\theta-v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^v,
\end{align}
with instantaneous correlation
\begin{equation}
    dW_t^S dW_t^v = \rho\,dt.
\end{equation}
The parameters $\kappa$, $\theta$, $\xi$, and $\rho$ denote the mean-reversion speed, long-run variance, volatility of volatility, and Brownian correlation, respectively.

Under this model, the option price depends on three variables:
\begin{equation}
    V = V(S,v,t).
\end{equation}
The corresponding Heston pricing PDE is
\begin{align}\label{eq:heston_pde}
\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
&+ \frac{1}{2}vS^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}
+ \rho\xi vS \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S \partial v}
+ \frac{1}{2}\xi^2 v \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial v^2} \notag \\
&+ rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
+ \kappa(\theta-v)\frac{\partial V}{\partial v}
- rV = 0.
\end{align}

Compared with the Black--Scholes PDE, the Heston equation introduces both an additional spatial dimension and a mixed derivative term. This makes grid-based numerical methods more demanding, because the computational cost grows rapidly as extra state variables are added. A PDE-theoretic treatment of the Black--Scholes equation under stochastic volatility is given by \textcite{ekstrom2010bs}. The Heston model is therefore a natural test case for PINNs, whose mesh-free formulation can sample the higher-dimensional domain without constructing a full tensor-product grid. Although the Heston PDE does not admit a simple closed-form solution, a semi-analytical benchmark is available through the Fourier-cosine (COS) method of \textcite{fang2008cos}, which exploits the known characteristic function of the Heston model; this benchmark is used in the Results section to validate the PINN. More recent work has moved beyond classical Markovian stochastic-volatility models toward ``rough'' volatility models driven by fractional Brownian motion \parencite{gatheral2018volatility,bayer2016rough,euch2019roughheston}; these models lie outside the scope of the present project.

\subsection{Theoretical foundations}\label{subsec:theory}

The PINN approach rests on two well-established results from the analysis of neural networks and modern scientific computing. The first is the universal approximation property. Feed-forward neural networks with at least one hidden layer can approximate any continuous function on a compact set to arbitrary precision, provided the activation function is non-polynomial and satisfies mild regularity conditions. \textcite{cybenko1989approximation} established this result for sigmoidal activations, and \textcite{hornik1989multilayer} extended it to the multilayer case with general squashing functions. Universal approximation is a necessary but not sufficient condition for PINNs: it guarantees that a network with sufficient capacity is in principle capable of representing the true solution, but says nothing about whether the optimizer actually finds such a configuration. For PINN training the requirement is also stronger than pointwise approximation of the price surface, since the PDE residual involves first- and second-order partial derivatives; smooth activation functions are therefore needed so that these derivatives are themselves well behaved. Modern mathematical treatments of approximation properties, derivative approximation, and deep-learning theory relevant to PDE-solving networks can be found in \parencite{jentzen2023mathintro,grohs2023mathematical,berner2021modern}.

The second foundation is automatic differentiation. The partial derivatives appearing in the PDE residual are not estimated by finite differences but computed directly through reverse-mode automatic differentiation on the computational graph of the network; see \textcite{baydin2018automatic} for a survey, and \parencite{paszke2019pytorch} for the PyTorch implementation used in this project. This is one reason smooth activations are required: the AD graph must support repeated differentiation, so that mixed and higher-order derivatives such as $\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial v$ can be evaluated stably. The same machinery makes the loss differentiable with respect to any quantity recorded in the graph, including the Heston parameters $\kappa$, $\theta$, $\xi$, and $\rho$ in the inverse problem, which allows the network weights and model parameters to be optimized jointly.

\subsection{Neural-network approximation}

Before introducing the full PINN loss, it is useful to specify the neural-network function class used to approximate the option-price surface. In this project, the unknown pricing function is represented by a fully connected feed-forward neural network, implemented in PyTorch using the \texttt{torch.nn.Module} interface \parencite{paszke2019pytorch,pytorch_docs}. The network is a multilayer perceptron composed of affine transformations, smooth nonlinear activation functions, and a final scalar output layer.

For the Black--Scholes model, the neural network takes the two-dimensional input $(S,\tau)$ and approximates the call-price surface as
\begin{equation}
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S,\tau) \approx V(S,\tau),
\end{equation}
whereas for the Heston model the input is extended to $(S,v,\tau)$:
\begin{equation}
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S,v,\tau) \approx V(S,v,\tau).
\end{equation}
Here $\boldsymbol{w}$ denotes the full collection of trainable weights and biases.

A fully connected network with $L$ hidden layers can be written recursively as
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{z}^{(0)} &= \mathbf{x}, \\
    \mathbf{z}^{(\ell)}
    &=
    \phi\!\left(
        W^{(\ell)}\mathbf{z}^{(\ell-1)}
        +
        \mathbf{b}^{(\ell)}
    \right),
    \qquad \ell=1,\ldots,L, \\
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x})
    &=
    W^{(L+1)}\mathbf{z}^{(L)}
    +
    \mathbf{b}^{(L+1)}.
\end{align}
For Black--Scholes, $\mathbf{x}=(S,\tau)$, while for Heston, $\mathbf{x}=(S,v,\tau)$. In the implementation, these inputs are normalized before being passed through the network. For example, the Heston network uses
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{\mathbf{x}}
    =
    \left(
        \frac{S}{S_{\max}},
        \frac{v}{v_{\max}},
        \frac{\tau}{T_{\max}}
    \right),
\end{equation}
which improves numerical conditioning by keeping the input variables on comparable scales.

The hidden layers are implemented using \texttt{nn.Linear} layers in PyTorch. In the Heston case, the network has the schematic form
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{R}^3
    \longrightarrow
    \mathbb{R}^{m}
    \longrightarrow
    \cdots
    \longrightarrow
    \mathbb{R}^{m}
    \longrightarrow
    \mathbb{R},
\end{equation}
where $m$ is the number of neurons per hidden layer. The scalar output is interpreted directly as the option value.

The activation functions considered in the hyperparameter sweeps were $\tanh$ and \texttt{softplus}, defined by
\begin{align}
    \tanh(x)
    &=
    \frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{e^x+e^{-x}}, \\
    \mathrm{softplus}(x)
    &=
    \log(1+e^x).
\end{align}
Both activation functions are smooth, which is important in the PINN setting because the PDE residual requires first-order, second-order, and mixed derivatives of the neural-network output. Non-smooth activations such as ReLU are less natural here, since their second derivatives are zero or undefined almost everywhere.

Weights were initialized using Xavier normal initialization \parencite{glorot2010xavier}, with biases initialized to zero. This initialization is standard for feed-forward neural networks \parencite{goodfellow2016deep} and helps prevent activations from becoming too large or too small at the start of training. The trainable parameters were optimized using the Adam optimizer \parencite{kingma2015adam}, and the final training runs used a cosine-annealing learning-rate schedule \parencite{loshchilov2017sgdr}. In simplified notation, the parameter update can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{w}_{n+1}
    =
    \boldsymbol{w}_n
    -
    \eta_n
    \widehat{\nabla_{\boldsymbol{w}} \mathcal{L}}(\boldsymbol{w}_n),
\end{equation}
where $\eta_n$ is the learning rate at iteration $n$ and $\widehat{\nabla_{\boldsymbol{w}} \mathcal{L}}$ denotes Adam's adaptive moment-based gradient estimate.

The final Black--Scholes PINN used two hidden layers, $256$ neurons per layer, $\tanh$ activation, and learning rate $5\times 10^{-4}$. The final Heston PINN used three hidden layers, $256$ neurons per layer, $\tanh$ activation, and learning rate $5\times 10^{-3}$. These choices were selected empirically from the hyperparameter sweeps reported in the Results section.

\subsection{Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)}

A PINN approximates the option-price function by a neural network
\begin{equation}
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S,\tau)
    \quad \text{or} \quad
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S,v,\tau),
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{w}$ denotes the trainable network weights. The network is trained not only to match payoff and boundary information, but also to satisfy the governing PDE in the interior of the domain.

The total loss is written as a weighted sum of PDE, initial-condition, and boundary-condition terms:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pinn_loss}
    \mathcal{L}
    =
    \lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{ic}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}.
\end{equation}
The PDE loss is computed as the mean squared residual of the relevant pricing equation:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_f}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N_f}
    \left|
        \mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}_i)
    \right|^2,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{R}_{\boldsymbol{w}}$ is the Black--Scholes or Heston PDE residual evaluated at interior collocation points $\mathbf{x}_i$. For the Black--Scholes model, $\mathbf{x}_i=(S_i,\tau_i)$, while for the Heston model, $\mathbf{x}_i=(S_i,v_i,\tau_i)$.

The initial-condition loss enforces the payoff at maturity:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{ic}}}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{ic}}}
    \left|
        V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S_i,0) - \max(S_i-K,0)
    \right|^2.
\end{equation}
The boundary-condition loss enforces the expected limiting behavior at the edges of the computational domain:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{bc}}}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{bc}}}
    \left|
        V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{bc}}_i)
        -
        g(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{bc}}_i)
    \right|^2,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{bc}}_i$ denotes a boundary collocation point and
$g(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{bc}}_i)$ is the prescribed boundary value at that point.
For the Black--Scholes call option, the asset-price boundaries are given by
\begin{align}
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(0,\tau) &\approx 0, \\
    V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S_{\max},\tau) &\approx S_{\max} - K e^{-r\tau}.
\end{align}
For the Heston model, analogous boundary penalties are imposed along both the
asset-price and variance boundaries.

All derivatives appearing in the PDE residuals are computed using automatic differentiation. This is central to the PINN approach: the model does not approximate derivatives using finite differences, but instead differentiates the neural network directly with respect to its inputs. As a result, first-order, second-order, mixed, and even higher-order sensitivities can be obtained from the same computational graph used during training.

PINNs sit within a broader family of deep-learning PDE solvers. Closely related approaches include the Deep Galerkin Method \parencite{sirignano2018dgm}, which minimizes a residual-based functional over sampled points in a manner conceptually similar to PINNs, and the deep BSDE / forward--backward SDE formulation that reframes the pricing problem as a stochastic control problem solved by neural networks \parencite{han2018solving,e2017deep}. Related Kolmogorov-PDE methods leverage Feynman--Kac representations to learn the solution from simulated paths \parencite{beck2021deep}, and \textcite{beck2020deepbsde} provide a recent overview of the deep BSDE family. Emerging alternative basis architectures such as Kolmogorov--Arnold Networks \parencite{liu2024kan} may also prove well suited to PDE tasks, but are not pursued in this project.

\subsection{Inverse Heston calibration}

In the inverse problem, the goal is no longer only to approximate the price surface for known parameters. Instead, selected Heston parameters are treated as trainable variables and recovered from synthetic market observations. The network is therefore optimized to satisfy three requirements simultaneously: fit observed option prices, respect the terminal and boundary conditions, and remain consistent with the Heston PDE.

For this setting, the loss function is extended by adding a market-data term:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse_loss}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{inv}}
    =
    \lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{ic}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{data}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_m}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N_m}
    \left|
        V_{\boldsymbol{w}}(S_i,v_i,\tau_i)
        -
        V_i^{\mathrm{market}}
    \right|^2.
\end{equation}
Here $V_i^{\mathrm{market}}$ denotes the observed or synthetic market price at the sampled point $(S_i,v_i,\tau_i)$. In this project, synthetic prices are generated from the Heston model and perturbed with Gaussian noise in order to mimic imperfect market observations, bid--ask effects, and calibration noise.

The inverse PINN can therefore be viewed as a differentiable calibration engine \parencite{dhiman2023pinns}. Since the PDE residual depends explicitly on $\kappa$, $\theta$, $\xi$, and $\rho$, gradients of the loss with respect to these parameters are obtained directly through automatic differentiation and optimized jointly with the network weights. After the main Adam training phase, the inverse calibration is refined using the limited-memory BFGS method of \textcite{liu1989limited}, which provides a quasi-Newton update suited for the smooth, low-curvature regime near the optimum.

\subsection{Greeks and automatic differentiation}

The Greeks measure the sensitivity of an option price to changes in state variables, time, and model parameters. In classical numerical implementations, these quantities are often estimated by bump-and-revalue procedures, where one input is perturbed and the pricing model is re-evaluated. This becomes increasingly expensive for mixed, higher-order, or model-specific sensitivities.

In the PINN framework, the Greeks are obtained directly by differentiating the trained neural-network price function. This is a natural by-product of the method: the same automatic-differentiation machinery used to construct the PDE residual is also used to compute price sensitivities. First-order, second-order, mixed, and third-order derivatives can therefore be extracted from the learned price surface without introducing a finite-difference stencil or repeatedly re-running the pricing model.

Since the project considers both the Black--Scholes and Heston models, two types of volatility sensitivity appear. In the Black--Scholes setting, volatility is a constant model parameter $\sigma$, so Vega-type quantities are derivatives with respect to $\sigma$. In the Heston setting, variance is itself a state variable $v$, so the corresponding sensitivities are derivatives with respect to $v$. The Greeks computed in this project are summarized in \cref{tab:greeks_expressions}.

The distinction between $\sigma$-Greeks and $v$-Greeks is important. In the Black--Scholes model, Vega, Vomma, Vanna, and Zomma describe sensitivity to the constant volatility parameter. In the Heston model, volatility enters through the variance state variable, and the corresponding quantities measure how the learned price surface changes along the variance dimension. Thus, the Heston quantities Vega$_v$, Volga$_v$, Vanna$_v$, and Zomma$_v$ should be interpreted as variance-state sensitivities rather than direct Black--Scholes volatility sensitivities.

\subsection{Collocation data and sampling strategy}

PINNs are mesh-free, but they are not data-free: their performance depends strongly on where collocation points are sampled. The collocation design therefore determines which regions of the pricing domain are emphasized during training. Since option-price surfaces contain the strongest curvature near the strike and near expiry, purely uniform sampling can underrepresent precisely the most difficult parts of the problem.

To address this, a domain-aware sampling strategy was used. Points were sampled throughout the full computational domain, with additional concentration near financially important regions: near the strike $S \approx K$, near the terminal condition $\tau \approx 0$, and, for the Heston model, near the initial variance level. This creates a richer training set around the kinked payoff and high-curvature regions while still exposing the network to the global structure of the solution.

\subsubsection{Black--Scholes forward problem}

For the Black--Scholes forward problem, the network is trained on interior collocation points, terminal payoff points, and asset-price boundary points. The asset-price domain is chosen wide enough to include deep out-of-the-money, at-the-money, and deep in-the-money behavior.

\subsubsection{Heston forward problem}

The Heston forward problem extends the input domain from $(S,\tau)$ to $(S,v,\tau)$. This increases the dimensionality of the learning problem and introduces the mixed derivative term $\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial v$ in the PDE residual. To maintain sufficient coverage of the enlarged domain, the number of interior collocation points is increased relative to the Black--Scholes case.

The generated Heston collocation set contains interior points, terminal payoff points, and candidate boundary points on the asset-price and variance boundaries. In the final Heston PINN, however, only the two asset-price boundaries are imposed in the boundary loss. The lower boundary uses the exact call-option condition
\begin{equation}
    V(0,v,\tau)=0,
\end{equation}
while the upper boundary uses the asymptotic approximation
\begin{equation}
    V(S_{\max},v,\tau)\approx S_{\max}-Ke^{-r\tau}.
\end{equation}
The variance-boundary points at $v=v_{\min}$ and $v=v_{\max}$ are generated but not imposed as hard zero-value boundary conditions, since the correct limiting behavior in the variance direction is less direct and should not be treated as $V=0$.

\subsection{Computational environment and GPU acceleration}

All numerical experiments were implemented in Python and executed primarily through cloud-based GPU runtimes. The use of Google Colab was essential for making the project computationally feasible, since Colab provides hosted Jupyter notebook environments with access to accelerated computing resources such as GPUs and TPUs \parencite{google_colab_faq}. In practice, the most demanding training runs were performed using high-performance NVIDIA data-center GPUs, including A100 and H100 accelerators.

This computational setup was particularly important for the Heston and inverse-calibration experiments. Compared with the Black--Scholes PINN, the Heston PINN requires a larger input domain, more collocation points, additional partial derivatives, and longer training horizons. The inverse problem is even more demanding because the neural-network weights and latent Heston parameters are optimized jointly. Without GPU acceleration, repeated architecture sweeps, learning-rate tests, loss-weight experiments, and long training runs would have been substantially slower and less practical.

The NVIDIA A100 Tensor Core GPU is designed for large-scale AI, data analytics, and high-performance computing workloads \parencite{nvidia_a100}. The NVIDIA H100 Tensor Core GPU extends this class of accelerator with Hopper-generation Tensor Cores and features aimed at modern deep-learning workloads \parencite{nvidia_h100}. Although this project does not require the scale of frontier model training, the same hardware characteristics are highly useful for PINNs: large batches of collocation points, repeated automatic differentiation, and dense neural-network optimization all benefit directly from GPU parallelism.

The computational workflow therefore consisted of local development and debugging on a personal machine, followed by larger training runs and hyperparameter sweeps in Colab GPU environments. This hybrid workflow made it possible to iterate quickly on code structure while still using cloud GPUs for the expensive training phases.
