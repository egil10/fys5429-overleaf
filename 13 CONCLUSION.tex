\section{Conclusion}\label{section:conclusion}

This project investigated Physics-Informed Neural Networks as mesh-free solvers for option-pricing partial differential equations, exploiting the fact that the Black--Scholes equation reduces to a heat equation under a standard change of variables and is therefore a natural target for the smooth, differentiable surrogates that PINNs produce. The work proceeded along three connected stages -- a forward solver for the Black--Scholes equation, a forward solver for the Heston stochastic-volatility PDE, and an inverse calibration of the Heston parameters $\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho$ jointly with the network weights $\boldsymbol{w}$ from noisy synthetic prices. The unifying theme is that a single differentiable price surrogate delivers prices and Greeks ``for free'' through the same automatic-differentiation machinery used to train it \parencite{baydin2018automatic,karniadakis2021physics}, and the same machinery also admits parameter calibration with no external solver.

The Black--Scholes experiment served as a controlled benchmark with a known closed form. At the at-the-money point $(S,\tau)=(K,T/2)=(100,0.5)$ the trained PINN predicted $V_{\mathrm{PINN}}=6.927159$ against $V_{\mathrm{BS}}=6.888729$, an absolute error of $0.038430$ ($0.56\%$ relative) and a local PDE residual $2.747\times 10^{-3}$ after $50{,}000$ Adam epochs (12\,min 8\,s on a single H100). The autodifferentiated Greeks (Delta, Theta, Gamma, Charm, Speed, Color) were qualitatively consistent with an at-the-money call. For a problem with a closed-form solution the value of the PINN is methodological rather than computational -- the closed form is faster and more accurate -- but the same machinery extends seamlessly to models without closed forms and to settings where Greeks across a full surface are needed without finite-difference approximations.

The Heston experiment extended the architecture to three dimensions $(S,v,\tau)$ with a PDE containing the mixed second derivative $\rho\xi vS\,\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial v$ and a variance-drift term. Boundary conditions were imposed on both $S=0$ and $S=S_{\max}$ (with $V(S_{\max},v,\tau)\approx S_{\max}-Ke^{-r\tau}$), and the network was trained for $50{,}000$ epochs on an A100 in 24\,min 40\,s. On the slice $v=v_0=0.04$ at $(S,\tau)=(100,0.5)$ the PINN predicted $V_{\mathrm{PINN}}=6.806941$ against the semi-analytical Heston benchmark $V_{\mathrm{Heston}}=6.837137$, an absolute error of $0.030196$ ($0.44\%$ relative). The local PDE residual was larger in magnitude ($-1.791\times 10^{-1}$) and the loss history was visibly noisier than in Black--Scholes, consistent with the standard observation that PINN optimisation landscapes degrade as the PDE becomes stiffer or more strongly coupled \parencite{wang2021understanding,wang2022expert,krishnapriyan2021characterizing}.

The inverse experiment recast the Heston PINN as a calibration engine: the four structural parameters were promoted to trainable variables, reparameterised through softplus and $\tanh$ bijections so that $\kappa,\theta,\xi>0$ and $\rho\in(-1,1)$ remained physical at every step, and optimised jointly with $\boldsymbol{w}$ against $200$ noisy synthetic prices using $25{,}000$ Adam epochs followed by $5{,}000$ L-BFGS steps (16\,min 17\,s) under loss weights $(\lambda_{\mathrm{pde}},\lambda_{\mathrm{ic}},\lambda_{\mathrm{bc}},\lambda_{\mathrm{data}},\lambda_{\mathrm{Feller}})=(20,10,5,1000,0.1)$. As a pricing fit, the calibration was extremely successful: mean absolute error $0.028566$, maximum absolute error $0.218854$, median relative error $\approx 0.02\%$, parity-plot $R^2=0.999999$. As a parameter-recovery problem, however, the same calibration failed. The recovered vector was $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)=(1.588,0.0266,0.104,0.994)$ against truth $(2.0,0.04,0.3,-0.7)$, and the most striking discrepancy is the correlation: $\rho$ converged to a strongly positive value despite the standard equity-market sign of the data-generating $\rho=-0.7$. Mechanistically this is not surprising. The parameter $\rho$ enters the Heston PDE only through the cross term $\rho\xi vS V_{Sv}$, and therefore appears in the price surface only through the implied-volatility skew that this term produces; with $200$ noisy points spread across a wide $(S,v,\tau)$ region the skew signal is far too weak to identify $\rho$ from prices alone. Moreover, $\rho$ always appears multiplied by $\xi$: the true product $\xi^\star\rho^\star=-0.21$, while the recovered $\xi\rho\approx+0.10$ -- not a clean sign flip but a structurally different basin of the data-likelihood landscape, in which a price-consistent representation is found through a different $(\xi,\rho)$ combination.

The financial implications are concrete. First, forward PINNs are already useful: once trained, the network is a fast differentiable pricing surrogate delivering prices and a full Greek surface through AD, directly applicable to pricing and hedging workflows. Second, inverse PINN calibration is a promising direction but is not yet competitive with classical calibration \parencite{andersen2008simple,rouah2013heston,lord2010comparison} as a black-box parameter-recovery tool. Third, the data deficit identified here is much less severe in practice: real-world calibration uses listed quotes across many strikes and maturities -- typically dense option chains in implied-volatility space -- which is orders of magnitude richer than the $200$ points used here, and it is precisely that density (especially the near-the-money skew) that classical calibration exploits to identify $\rho$ \parencite{gatheral2018volatility,horvath2021deep}. Fourth, PINNs are likely to be most valuable in settings where classical approaches are not available, in particular rough-volatility models \parencite{bayer2016rough,euch2019roughheston} for which no easily-invertible characteristic function exists.

\paragraph{Cost trade-off.} For a single quote the PINN is uncompetitive: the $\approx 25$-minute Heston training is several orders of magnitude slower than a sub-second Crank--Nicolson finite-difference solve on a moderate $(S,v,\tau)$ grid \parencite{tavella2000pricing,achdou2005computational}. The economics flip when a trained surface is queried millions of times -- Greeks across a whole portfolio, or a hedging simulator with continuous re-evaluations -- at which point the marginal cost of an additional query is one forward pass plus one AD pass, well below a millisecond. The break-even is roughly $10^{6}$ price-and-Greek queries against a vectorised Crank--Nicolson reference and shifts further in favour of the PINN as model dimensionality grows or when training has to be repeated across calibration scenarios.

Several concrete extensions follow from these experiments. (i) Richer collocation and observation designs spanning a full strike--maturity surface with controlled noise levels and near-the-money refinement to expose the skew that pins down $\rho$. (ii) Bayesian or regularised priors on $\rho$ and $\xi$ encoding the strong economic prior $\rho<0$ for equity underlyings, turning the under-identified calibration into a well-posed MAP problem. (iii) Loss functions formulated in implied-volatility space rather than price space, so that the skew signal that identifies $\rho$ is amplified relative to the level signal that dominates a price-MSE loss. (iv) Extension of the forward solver to American and exotic options where the mesh-free property of the PINN pays off most clearly. (v) Extension to rough Heston \parencite{euch2019roughheston} as the natural target where no closed-form characteristic function is easily inverted and where a differentiable surrogate is structurally hardest to beat.

The overall picture is that PINNs are a powerful and flexible differentiable framework for financial PDEs. Forward pricing is in good shape across both Black--Scholes and Heston, and the same machinery delivers Greeks essentially for free. Inverse calibration is genuinely harder and more delicate: a near-perfect price fit does not imply structural parameter recovery, and this project demonstrates both sides of that trade-off in a single end-to-end pipeline.
