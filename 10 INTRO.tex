\section{Introduction}\label{section:Introduction}

Option pricing is one of the clearest examples of how ideas from mathematical physics enter modern finance \parencite{hjorthjensen2015computational}. At the centre of this connection lies the heat equation, the canonical partial differential equation governing diffusion. In one spatial dimension,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heat-equation}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x,t)=\alpha\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}(x,t),
\end{equation}
where $u(x,t)$ is the state variable, $x$ a spatial coordinate, $t$ time, and $\alpha>0$ a diffusion coefficient. The equation says that local changes in the field are driven by spatial curvature, smoothing irregularities over time, and the same parabolic structure arises, after a suitable change of variables, in option-pricing PDEs.

The classical Black--Scholes model \parencite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational,capinski2012blackscholes} is the canonical example. Under geometric Brownian motion the value of a European option $V(S,t)$ satisfies a parabolic PDE whose second-order term describes diffusion in the underlying asset price, and admits a probabilistic interpretation as a discounted risk-neutral expected payoff \parencite{shreve2004stochastic,profeta2010option}. The log-moneyness transformation $x=\ln(S/K)$ and time-to-maturity $\tau=T-t$ reduce the Black--Scholes equation to a heat equation \parencite{capinski2012blackscholes}, so option pricing is literally backward diffusion of the terminal payoff into a smooth continuation-value surface. For a European call and put the payoff at expiry is
\begin{align}
    C(S,0) &= \max(S-K,0), \\
    P(S,0) &= \max(K-S,0),
\end{align}
which is continuous but not differentiable at the strike (\cref{fig:black2d.pdf}). This kink is a localised singularity in curvature that must be propagated backward through the smoothing PDE. \Cref{fig:black3d.pdf} shows the resulting surface: smooth for $\tau>0$, with the kinked terminal cross-section at $\tau=0$.

This project investigates Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) as mesh-free solvers for option-pricing PDEs. Introduced by \textcite{raissi2019physics}, building on \parencite{raissi2017physics1}, PINNs embed the governing differential equation directly into the training objective of a neural network: the network is penalised whenever its output violates the PDE, boundary conditions, or terminal payoff, so the learned function is constrained to behave like a solution of the underlying financial model rather than merely interpolating prices. 

The methodology has matured into an active subfield of scientific machine learning, with recent reviews \parencite{karniadakis2021physics,cuomo2022scientific} and dedicated software libraries \parencite{lu2021deepxde}. Its theoretical backbone is the use of a neural network as a universal function approximator \parencite{cybenko1989approximation,hornik1989multilayer,pinkus1999approximation}; modern mathematical treatments of deep learning \parencite{jentzen2023mathintro,grohs2023mathematical,berner2021modern} provide rigorous foundations, and convergence/generalisation analyses tailored to PINNs on parabolic problems are emerging \parencite{shin2020convergence,mishra2022estimates}.

PINNs are conceptually attractive for option pricing. Classical finite-difference and finite-element methods discretise the computational domain explicitly \parencite{tavella2000pricing,achdou2005computational}, which is powerful in low dimensions but increasingly expensive as more state variables are introduced. PINNs instead evaluate the PDE residual at sampled collocation points; the network itself represents the continuous solution, so no fixed tensor-product grid is required. This mesh-free property places PINNs within a broader family of deep-learning PDE solvers including the Deep Galerkin Method \parencite{sirignano2018dgm}, deep-BSDE methods \parencite{han2018solving,e2017deep,beck2020deepbsde}, and deep Kolmogorov solvers \parencite{beck2021deep}. Once trained, the same network supplies prices and Greeks through automatic differentiation \parencite{baydin2018automatic,paszke2019pytorch} and is therefore a differentiable surrogate that supports gradient-based calibration \parencite{ruf2020neural,germain2021neural}.

Real markets exhibit features, volatility smiles, term-structure effects, and fat tails, that the constant-volatility Black--Scholes model cannot capture \parencite{gatheral2018volatility,fouque2011multiscale,hull2018options,wilmott1995mathematics}. The Heston model \parencite{heston1993closed} treats variance as a stochastic state variable, so the option price depends on asset price, instantaneous variance, and time, $V=V(S,v,t)$. The resulting PDE contains an additional spatial dimension and a mixed-derivative term arising from the asset--variance correlation \parencite{ekstrom2010bs}, which makes it substantially more demanding than Black--Scholes and a meaningful test of PINNs on realistic financial models; rough-volatility extensions \parencite{bayer2016rough,euch2019roughheston,euch2018perfect} lie beyond the scope of this report but are mentioned for completeness. 

Beyond the forward problem, PINNs also offer a natural framework for inverse calibration: option prices are observed but model parameters are latent, and traditional calibration is a nonlinear optimisation over repeated forward solves \parencite{andersen2008simple,lord2010comparison,rouah2013heston}. Because the PINN pricing surrogate and its derivatives are differentiable with respect to both inputs and parameters, calibration can be performed by joint AD-based optimisation, which is increasingly explored across quantitative finance \parencite{bayer2019deep,horvath2021deep,glau2022deep,lewis2025pinnsoptions,dhiman2023pinns}.

The objectives of this project are therefore threefold.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Use the Black--Scholes equation as a controlled benchmark where the PINN solution can be compared against the closed-form price.
    \item Extend the same framework to the three-dimensional Heston stochastic-volatility PDE and evaluate it against a semi-analytical Fourier-quadrature reference.
    \item Adapt the Heston PINN to an inverse setting in which latent model parameters are recovered from noisy synthetic market prices.
\end{enumerate}

The main quantitative findings are as follows. The Black--Scholes PINN reproduces the analytical price surface with a relative error of $\approx 0.56\%$ at the at-the-money point ($V_{\mathrm{PINN}}=6.927$ versus $V_{\mathrm{BS}}=6.889$) and a local PDE residual of order $10^{-3}$. The Heston PINN, trained on a three-dimensional $(S,v,\tau)$ domain with the mixed-derivative term included, attains $V_{\mathrm{PINN}}=6.807$ against the semi-analytical Heston benchmark $V_{\mathrm{Heston}}=6.837$, a relative error of $\approx 0.44\%$. The inverse Heston PINN fits $200$ noisy synthetic option prices with $R^2\approx 1.000$ and median relative pricing error $\approx 0.02\%$, but recovers the structural parameters $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)$ only partially. Most strikingly, the Brownian correlation $\rho$ converges to a large positive value despite the true value being $\rho=-0.7$. This is the central pedagogical finding of the project: a near-perfect price fit does not imply structural parameter recovery, because $\rho$ enters the Heston PDE only through the mixed term $\rho\xi vS V_{Sv}$ and is therefore identifiable from prices only through implied-volatility skew that $200$ scattered points cannot carry.

The remainder of the report is organised as follows. \Cref{section:methods} develops the model chain from the heat equation to the Black--Scholes and Heston PDEs, formulates the PINN composite-loss objective, presents the AD pipeline, reparameterisation of constrained Heston parameters, two-stage Adam + L-BFGS schedule, collocation strategy, and computational environment, with the algorithm and the key reparameterisation listing inline. \Cref{section:result} reports the three experiments in order of increasing difficulty: Black--Scholes forward against the closed-form benchmark, Heston forward against the semi-analytical benchmark, and inverse Heston calibration on synthetic noisy prices. \Cref{section:conclusion} summarises the findings, situates them against classical calibration practice, and identifies four directions for follow-up work. The appendix collects the stochastic-process sample paths, the full per-component loss histories, the ranked hyperparameter-sweep panels, and the inverse calibration diagnostics. All code, generated collocation data, and trained checkpoints are openly available \parencite{furnes2026fys5429code,furnes2026fys5429overleaf}.
