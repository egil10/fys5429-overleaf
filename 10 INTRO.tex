\section{Introduction}\label{section:Introduction}

Option pricing is one of the clearest examples of how ideas from mathematical physics enter modern finance \parencite{hjorthjensen2015computational}. At the centre of this connection lies the heat equation, the canonical partial differential equation governing diffusion. In one spatial dimension, the heat equation is given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heat-equation}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x,t)
    =
    \alpha
    \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}(x,t),
\end{equation}
where $u(x,t)$ denotes the state variable, $x$ the spatial coordinate, $t$ time, and $\alpha>0$ the diffusion coefficient. The equation states that local changes in the field are driven by spatial curvature: sharp irregularities are progressively smoothed out over time. This diffusion mechanism is not only central in physics, but also appears, after a suitable transformation, in the mathematical structure of option-pricing PDEs.

The classical Black--Scholes model \parencite{black1973pricing,merton1973rational,capinski2012blackscholes}, is the most important example of this connection. Under geometric Brownian motion, the value of a European option, $V(S,t)$, satisfies a parabolic PDE whose second-order term describes diffusion in the underlying asset price, and admits a clean probabilistic interpretation as a discounted expected payoff through the standard stochastic-calculus derivation \parencite{shreve2004stochastic,profeta2010option}. By introducing the log-moneyness transformation
\begin{equation}
    x = \ln\left(\frac{S}{K}\right)
\end{equation}
and reversing time through the time-to-maturity variable
\begin{equation}
    \tau = T-t,
\end{equation}
the Black--Scholes equation can be reduced to a heat-equation form \parencite{capinski2012blackscholes}. In this sense, option pricing may be interpreted as a diffusion problem: the terminal payoff is propagated backward through time into a smooth continuation-value surface.

This interpretation is especially clear when considering the payoff at expiry. At $\tau=0$, a European option has no remaining time value, and its price is determined entirely by intrinsic value. For a European call and put, the payoff functions are shown below
\begin{align}
    C(S,0) &= \max(S-K,0), \\
    P(S,0) &= \max(K-S,0).
\end{align}
Both payoffs are continuous but not differentiable at the strike price. This non-smooth point is financially important because it separates out-of-the-money and in-the-money regions; mathematically, it creates a localized singularity in curvature. \cref{fig:black2d.pdf} shows this terminal cross-section.

The numerical challenge is therefore already visible at the terminal condition: a pricing method must learn a function that begins with a sharp kink but becomes smooth as time to maturity increases. Moving backward from expiry, the parabolic structure of the Black--Scholes PDE diffuses the terminal payoff. The option price surface is consequently smooth for $\tau>0$, with the continuation value emerging from the interaction between uncertainty, time, and discounting. This smoothing effect is illustrated in \cref{fig:black3d.pdf}, where the two-dimensional payoff curve expands into a full price surface over asset price and time to maturity.

This project investigates Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) as mesh-free solvers for such option-pricing PDEs. Introduced by \textcite{raissi2019physics}, building on the earlier preprint \parencite{raissi2017physics1}, PINNs embed the governing differential equation directly into the training objective of a neural network. Rather than fitting only observed data, the network is penalized whenever its output violates the PDE, boundary conditions, or terminal payoff condition. The learned function is therefore constrained not merely to interpolate prices, but to behave like a solution of the underlying financial model. The methodology has since matured into an active subfield of scientific machine learning, with comprehensive recent reviews \parencite{karniadakis2021physics,cuomo2022scientific} and dedicated software libraries \parencite{lu2021deepxde}. At its core a PINN is a neural network used as a universal function approximator, and modern mathematical treatments of deep learning provide a rigorous foundation for this perspective \parencite{jentzen2023mathintro,grohs2023mathematical,berner2021modern}.

This is conceptually attractive for option pricing. Classical finite-difference and finite-element methods rely on an explicit discretization of the computational domain \parencite{tavella2000pricing,achdou2005computational}. Such methods are powerful in low dimensions, but they become increasingly expensive as more state variables are introduced. PINNs instead evaluate the PDE residual at sampled collocation points. Since the network itself represents the continuous solution, no fixed tensor-product grid is required. This mesh-free property makes PINNs particularly appealing for models beyond Black--Scholes, and PINNs themselves sit within a broader landscape of deep-learning approaches to high-dimensional PDEs, including the Deep Galerkin Method \parencite{sirignano2018dgm}, deep BSDE methods \parencite{han2018solving,e2017deep}, and deep Kolmogorov solvers \parencite{beck2021deep}, recently surveyed in \textcite{beck2020deepbsde,germain2021neural}. Rigorous convergence and generalization results for PINNs on parabolic problems are also beginning to emerge \parencite{shin2020convergence,mishra2022estimates}.

The same underlying neural-network technology has driven progress across many other domains, from attention-based language models \parencite{vaswani2017attention} and recurrent sequence learners such as LSTMs \parencite{hochreiter1997lstm} to adversarial generative modeling \parencite{goodfellow2014gan}. Even more recent work explores alternative basis-function architectures such as Kolmogorov--Arnold Networks \parencite{liu2024kan}. The present project uses a standard fully connected architecture for option pricing, but this broader literature offers many alternatives that could in principle be adapted to financial PDEs.

Mathematically tractable models such as Black--Scholes form the foundation of derivative pricing \parencite{hull2018options,wilmott1995mathematics}, but real markets exhibit features --- volatility smiles, term-structure effects, fat tails --- that demand richer model classes \parencite{gatheral2018volatility,fouque2011multiscale}. As soon as one moves beyond closed-form models, the computational burden shifts onto numerical PDE methods \parencite{tavella2000pricing,achdou2005computational}. Classical schemes scale poorly with the number of state variables, and calibration to observed prices typically requires repeated forward solves inside a nonlinear least-squares loop \parencite{andersen2008simple,lord2010comparison}. A mesh-free, differentiable surrogate for the price function that simultaneously provides Greeks and supports gradient-based calibration is therefore appealing --- and this is precisely what a PINN delivers; see \textcite{ruf2020neural,germain2021neural} for broader reviews of neural-network methods in option pricing and hedging.

The Heston model provides the main higher-dimensional test case in this report. Unlike Black--Scholes, which assumes constant volatility, the Heston model treats variance as a stochastic state variable \parencite{fouque2011multiscale}. The option price therefore depends not only on asset price and time, but also on instantaneous variance:
\begin{equation}
    V = V(S,v,t).
\end{equation}
The resulting PDE contains an additional spatial dimension and a mixed derivative term arising from the correlation between asset-price and variance shocks; a modern PDE-theoretic treatment of this and related option-pricing equations is given in \textcite{ekstrom2010bs}. This makes the Heston equation substantially more demanding than the Black--Scholes PDE and therefore a more meaningful test of whether PINNs can handle realistic financial models. An important recent direction in stochastic-volatility modeling replaces standard Brownian variance drivers with fractional ones, giving rise to so-called rough volatility models \parencite{bayer2016rough,euch2019roughheston,euch2018perfect}; these are beyond the scope of this project but are mentioned here for completeness.

Beyond the forward pricing problem, PINNs also offer a natural framework for inverse calibration. In market applications, option prices are observed, but model parameters are latent. For the Heston model, quantities such as the mean-reversion speed $\kappa$, long-run variance $\theta$, volatility of volatility $\xi$, and correlation $\rho$ must be inferred from option-price data \parencite{rouah2013heston}. Traditional calibration is typically formulated as a nonlinear optimization problem over repeated pricing evaluations \parencite{andersen2008simple,lord2010comparison}. A PINN provides a different approach: because the pricing function and its derivatives are differentiable with respect to both inputs and parameters, calibration can be performed by optimizing the model parameters directly through automatic differentiation \parencite{baydin2018automatic,paszke2019pytorch}. More broadly, deep learning is increasingly used across quantitative finance, with applications ranging from limit-order-book modeling \parencite{zhang2019deeplob}, deep calibration of (rough) volatility models \parencite{bayer2019deep,horvath2021deep,glau2022deep}, to PINN-based derivative pricing and calibration \parencite{lewis2025pinnsoptions,dhiman2023pinns}.

The objective of this project is therefore threefold.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item First, the Black--Scholes equation is used as a benchmark problem where the PINN solution can be compared against a closed-form analytical price.
    \item Second, the framework is extended to the Heston stochastic-volatility PDE and evaluated against a semi-analytical reference solution.
    \item Third, the Heston PINN is adapted to an inverse setting in which latent model parameters are recovered from noisy synthetic market prices.
\end{enumerate}

The main quantitative findings of this work are as follows. The Black--Scholes PINN reproduces the analytical price surface with a relative error of $\approx 0.56\%$ at the at-the-money point and a local PDE residual of order $10^{-3}$. The Heston PINN, trained on a three-dimensional domain $(S, v, \tau)$ with the mixed derivative term included, achieves a relative error of $\approx 0.44\%$ at the at-the-money point against a semi-analytical Heston benchmark. The inverse Heston PINN fits 200 noisy synthetic option prices with $R^2 \approx 1.000$ and median relative error $\approx 0.02\%$, but recovers the structural Heston parameters only partially --- notably, the Brownian correlation $\rho$ converges to a large positive value despite the true value being $\rho = -0.7$. This last finding illustrates a generic identifiability difficulty in inverse calibration that has direct implications for practical Heston calibration in equity option markets.

The remainder of the report is organized as follows. \cref{section:methods} (Methods) develops the model chain from the heat equation to the Black--Scholes and Heston PDEs, introduces the PINN composite-loss formulation, automatic-differentiation pipeline, collocation sampling strategy, and computational environment, and gives explicit algorithmic and code listings of the training procedure. \cref{section:result} (Results) presents the three experiments in order of increasing difficulty: the Black--Scholes forward benchmark with closed-form ground truth, the Heston forward solution with semi-analytical ground truth, and the inverse Heston calibration with synthetic noisy market prices. \cref{section:conclusion} (Conclusion) summarises the main findings, discusses limitations, and points to concrete extensions. The appendix (\cref{section:appendix}) collects the stochastic-process sample paths, full per-component loss histories, the hyperparameter-sweep panels, and the inverse calibration diagnostics.
