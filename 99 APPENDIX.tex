\subsection{Black--Scholes PINN}

The Black--Scholes hyperparameter study consists of four $10{,}000$-epoch sweeps on the standard collocation grid: architecture, Adam learning rate, loss weights, and epoch budget. The ranked summaries and training curves tell the same story. \texttt{tanh} dominates \texttt{softplus}; at least 128 neurons are needed for sub-$10^{-2}$ residuals; $\eta_0=5\times10^{-4}$ sits in the stable region before large learning rates begin to oscillate; PDE-heavy loss weights perform best; and the residual is still decaying at $10{,}000$ epochs, motivating the final $50{,}000$-epoch run.

\subsection{Inverse PINN}

The inverse PINN was trained on a synthetic Heston dataset of $200$ noisy price observations spread across the $(S,v,\tau)$ domain, with the four Heston parameters reparameterized via softplus/\texttt{tanh} (Listing~\ref{lst:reparam}). Optimization used Adam for $25{,}000$ epochs followed by $5{,}000$ L-BFGS refinement steps \parencite{liu1989limited}, with $(\lambda_{\text{PDE}},\lambda_{\text{IC}},\lambda_{\text{BC}},\lambda_{\text{data}},\lambda_{\text{Feller}})=(20,10,5,1000,0.1)$, NN learning rate $\eta_0=2\times10^{-3}$, and Heston-parameter learning rate $\eta_{\text{Heston}}=10^{-3}$. The loss panel shows successful optimization of the pricing objective: the data loss drops below $10^{-2}$ by epoch $\sim20{,}000$ and the Feller penalty remains at machine zero throughout. The parameter panel shows the opposite conclusion structurally: the final calibrated values $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)=(1.588,0.0266,0.104,0.994)$ differ sharply from the data-generating truth $(2.0,0.04,0.3,-0.7)$ in Table~\ref{tab:inverse_pinn_calibration_history}, with $\rho$ converging to the wrong sign.

\subsubsection{Final calibration report}

The final calibration report collects the main diagnostics of the inverse Heston experiment in a single figure. Taken at face value, the pricing fit looks highly successful: predicted prices track the synthetic market prices closely, the error distribution is tightly centered around zero, and the maturity-slice comparison at $\tau\approx0.5$ shows that the learned pricing function reproduces the observed option-price curve well across asset prices. In other words, as a differentiable pricing surrogate, the inverse PINN performs very well.

The structural conclusion is different. The parameter comparison shows that accurate price fitting does not imply correct recovery of the data-generating Heston parameters. The final estimates $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)=(1.588,0.0266,0.104,0.994)$ remain biased relative to the true values $(2.0,0.04,0.3,-0.7)$, most notably because $\rho$ is recovered with the wrong sign. This is the clearest illustration of the identifiability problem in the report: in the Heston PDE, $\rho$ enters only through the mixed term $\rho\xi v S\,\partial^2V/\partial S\partial v$, so it is identified mainly through leverage-skew information in the option surface. With only $200$ noisy observations spread across a broad variance domain, that skew signal is too weak to force the optimizer onto the true negative-correlation branch.
